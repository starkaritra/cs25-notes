Shaping AI from the History of the Transformer — Hyung Won Chung

A scaling-minded research compass: find the dominant force, ride it, and remove assumptions once they become bottlenecks.

🎓 Hyung Won Chung (OpenAI) ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Hyung Won Chung is not giving a catalog of Transformer variants. He is teaching a way to choose research bets when AI moves too quickly to memorize every new result. His claim is deliberately opinionated: the dominant driver is exponentially cheaper compute, so good AI research often means building methods that can absorb more compute, data, and freedom.

Running analogy — removing scaffolding from a growing bridge

Early in construction, scaffolding is useful: it holds pieces in place before the bridge can support itself. But if the bridge grows and the scaffolding stays bolted on, the support becomes a cage. In this note, hand-designed model structure is the scaffolding; scaling is the bridge getting stronger.

Epistemic status

This is a research taste lecture, not a theorem. The compute trend and Transformer designs are factual; the extrapolation — which assumptions are now expired — is a practitioner intuition meant to guide bets, not prove inevitability.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these first. Each item is a hook the lecture reuses rather than reteaches.

  • Compute / scaling — if dollars buy more FLOPs over time, models can become larger or train longer; see Note 13 for scaling laws and emergence.
  • Inductive bias — an assumption baked into the model, such as “inputs and outputs deserve separate stacks”; it can reduce sample cost now and limit freedom later.
  • The bitter lesson — Sutton's phrase for the repeated win of general methods that exploit computation over hand-coded human structure.
  • Next-token prediction — train by predicting token \(x_t\) from context \(x_{<t}\); the simple objective can incentivize many latent skills. Cross-link: Note 27.
  • RL for reasoning — reward a model for producing solutions that score well, rather than hand-writing the reasoning procedure; relevant as a modern “incentivize, don’t teach” extension.

1.Study the change, not every headline

Chung opens with a student-facing problem: if AI advances every week, “keeping up” by chasing each latest model, agent, or benchmark becomes impossible. His proposed move is to study the change itself: identify the dominant driving force, understand it, then roll it forward to guess the direction of travel.

1.1 The pen-drop test

He illustrates prediction with a pen. Many forces act on it — gravity, drag, complicated aerodynamics — but if the pen is heavy enough, gravity dominates. Once you isolate that force and use Newtonian mechanics, a rough prediction becomes easy.

Key equation — when one force dominates

$$y(t)=y_0+v_0t+\frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

Here \(y(t)\) is vertical position, \(y_0\) the starting position, \(v_0\) the starting velocity, and \(g\) gravitational acceleration. Chung's point is not physics; it is methodology: when one force dominates, prediction becomes less mystical.

1.2 The dominant force in AI: cheaper compute

For AI, Chung points to Rich Sutton's long-run chart of calculations per dollar: the amount of compute available for the same money rises roughly exponentially. His practical inference is stark: do not compete with this trend; leverage it. Human cleverness improves, but not as reliably as the compute-per-dollar curve.

✓ Check yourself — what is the pen-drop analogy doing?

It is not claiming AI is as predictable as mechanics. It says: if a narrow domain has one dominant driver, you can often predict direction better than chance by understanding that driver.

2.The bitter lesson: today's shortcut can become tomorrow's bottleneck

The lecture's central research principle is Sutton's bitter lesson, in Chung's wording: decades of AI progress look like a march toward more general methods, weaker modeling assumptions, more data, and more compute. Hand-engineered structure can be productive at today's scale, but it often restricts the model exactly when tomorrow's scale arrives.

2.1 Structure helps, then caps

Imagine two bridges. The scaffolded bridge opens sooner, because the supports make construction easy. The self-supporting bridge looks unstable at first, because it has more degrees of freedom. But if traffic keeps increasing, the bolted-on scaffolding becomes the height limit.

Key equation — a toy scaling law plus a ceiling

$$P_{\text{general}}(C)=a+b\log_{10}C \qquad P_{\text{structured}}(C)=\min\left(L,\,a_s+b_s\log_{10}C\right)$$

\(C\) is compute, \(P\) is task performance, and \(L\) is the ceiling introduced by an assumption. This is a teaching toy, not a claimed empirical law: it encodes Chung's qualitative curve.

2.2 What is better long-term may look worse now

Chung emphasizes a psychologically hard implication: a more scalable method can underperform today. If it gives the model more freedom, it may look chaotic at small compute. The right choice depends on where the field sits on the compute curve — use scaffolding when needed, but remember to remove it.

Common misconception

“Bitter lesson means never add structure.” Chung says the opposite nuance: add the structure needed at the current compute frontier, but treat it as temporary scaffolding. The mistake is forgetting to unbolt it later.

✓ Check yourself — why can a worse-looking method be the better research bet?

Because small-scale performance measures current usefulness, not scalability. A method with fewer assumptions may need more compute before it crosses over, then continue improving after the structured method has plateaued.

3.Worked micro-example — when added structure becomes a bottleneck

Make the lecture's curve concrete. Suppose two translation systems are tested at three compute scales. The structured model has a hand-designed bias that helps at low compute but caps what it can represent. The general model wastes compute early but keeps benefiting from scale.

Compute scaleStructured model accuracyGeneral model accuracyWinner now
1×60%45%Structured by 15 points
10×72%70%Structured by 2 points
100×75%88%General by 13 points
Step-by-step comparison

At \(1\times\) compute: $$60-45=15\text{ points}$$ so the structure is useful.

At \(10\times\) compute: $$72-70=2\text{ points}$$ so the advantage nearly disappears.

At \(100\times\) compute: $$88-75=13\text{ points}$$ so the general method has crossed over. The structured model improved only \(75-60=15\) points total, while the general model improved \(88-45=43\) points.

Lesson for what to build today

If your method wins only because it bakes in a narrow assumption, ask: is this a durable advantage, or a low-compute crutch? A good scaling bet should have a credible path to keep improving as \(C\) grows.

✓ Check yourself — at what scale does the research decision flip?

Between \(10\times\) and \(100\times\). At \(10\times\), structured still wins by 2 points; at \(100\times\), general wins by 13 points. The crossover is the warning sign Chung wants researchers to anticipate.

Bitter lesson as a research compass

Do not ask only “what structure improves today's benchmark?” Ask “which assumptions are becoming removable because compute, data, and algorithms have changed?” That question turns history into a compass rather than nostalgia.

✓ Check yourself — name one assumption in the table that was reasonable before it became costly.

Example: bidirectional encoders were excellent for 2018-style language-understanding benchmarks such as SQuAD/GLUE, but they complicate modern multi-turn generation because old tokens may need to be re-encoded instead of cached.

5.Transformer history as a case study in assumption removal

After the scaling thesis, Chung gets concrete: compare the original encoder-decoder Transformer, BERT-style encoder-only models, and GPT-style decoder-only models. They are not merely different diagrams; each encodes a bet about what structure is worth imposing.

5.1 Three architectures, one sequence-model family

At first principles, a Transformer is a sequence model: tokenize a sequence, embed tokens as vectors, and let tokens interact through attention. The variants differ in which tokens may talk and whether input and output use separate machinery.

5.2 Encoder-decoder assumptions Chung revisits

Chung walks through how to transform encoder-decoder toward decoder-only: share cross-attention machinery with self-attention, share parameters between source and target, align attention layer-by-layer rather than only to the final encoder layer, and remove bidirectional input attention. Each step removes a scaffold.

  • Separate input/target parameters. Reasonable for English-to-German translation; unnatural when one model stores shared world knowledge across languages, tasks, and chat turns.
  • Decoder attends to final encoder layer. Convenient design, but possibly a depth mismatch if models become hundreds or thousands of layers deep.
  • Bidirectional input attention. Powerful for BERT-era understanding tasks, but costly for multi-turn chat because caching is harder: earlier tokens may need re-encoding when new tokens arrive.
  • Encoder-only classification. Great for GLUE-era progress, but it gives up generation — too restrictive for the most general use cases.
Key equation — decoder-only unifies supervised learning as text

$$P_\theta(y\mid x)=\prod_{t=1}^{T}P_\theta(y_t\mid x, y_{<t})$$

\(x\) is the prompt or input, \(y_t\) is the \(t\)-th target token, and the same causal stack handles both “read the input” and “write the answer.” The architecture does not need a separate cross-attention module to express the supervised task.

Common misconception

“Decoder-only means it can only do unsupervised language modeling.” Chung explicitly rejects this. Concatenate input and target, train the causal model to predict the target tokens, and supervised learning becomes sequence modeling.

✓ Check yourself — why did Flan-T5 benefit strongly from instruction tuning in Chung's anecdote?

His hypothesis: many academic instruction-tuning tasks had long inputs and short targets. Encoder-decoder's separate input/target structure matched that data shape well, so the scaffold was useful in that regime.

6.Research taste under scaling

The talk ends by turning architectural history into advice for students: look at your problem, list the assumptions you are making, and ask whether they are still necessary. If not, test the more general version and scale it.

6.1 Don't teach; incentivize

Chung criticizes the instinct to model “how we think” and then teach that structure to the machine. We do not understand our own cognition at the low level well enough. The scaling-friendly alternative is to create objectives that incentivize useful behavior: next-token prediction incentivizes grammar, facts, and translation; instruction tuning incentivizes following requests; RL-style reasoning training can incentivize successful solution search without hand-coding the solver.

Key equation — objective as incentive

$$\theta^*=\arg\max_\theta\;\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim \pi_\theta}\big[R(\tau)\big]$$

\(\tau\) is a generated trajectory, \(R(\tau)\) is a reward such as correctness or preference, and \(\pi_\theta\) is the model policy. The equation says: do not specify every internal step; reward trajectories that achieve the goal. Treat this as a modern extension of the lecture's scaling intuition, not a claim that reward design is solved.

6.2 What to build now

Chung's student takeaway is not “copy GPT.” It is a habit:

  1. Name the dominant force. In this lecture: compute per dollar and associated scaling.
  2. Name your scaffolding. What did you assume because today's compute, data, benchmarks, or evaluation made it convenient?
  3. Predict the expiration date. What happens at \(10\times\), \(100\times\), or \(1000\times\) more compute?
  4. Build the removal path. Design experiments where the more general method can catch up and cross over.
Connection to emergence

Note 13 explains why capabilities can look absent until scale crosses a threshold. Chung's addition is a research strategy: if the threshold will arrive, avoid architectures whose assumptions prevent the model from benefiting when it does.

✓ Check yourself — write the scaling-research question in one sentence.

“Which assumption helped because we lacked compute, and can we now remove it to make the method more general and scalable?”

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Dominant driving forceThe main cause that explains the direction of change well enough to guide predictions.
Compute per dollarHow many calculations a fixed budget buys; Chung treats its exponential improvement as AI's central tailwind.
Inductive biasA modeling assumption that makes some solutions easier to learn than others.
Bitter lessonThe historical pattern that general methods exploiting computation beat approaches that encode human structure too tightly.
Encoder-decoderA Transformer with one stack for the input sequence and another for the output sequence, connected by cross-attention.
Encoder-onlyA bidirectional Transformer used mainly to map a sequence to a representation or label.
Decoder-onlyA causal Transformer stack that predicts the next token and can represent prompts, inputs, targets, and chat history in one sequence.
Cross-attentionAn attention module where decoder tokens attend to representations produced by an encoder.
CachingReusing previously computed token states during generation instead of recomputing the whole context.
RL-style incentiveTraining that rewards successful trajectories rather than specifying the internal procedure by hand.
