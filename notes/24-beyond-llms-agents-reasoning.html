Beyond LLMs: Agents, Emergent Abilities, Intermediate-Guided Reasoning & BabyLM

A survey of four frontiers where the field moves past “make the language model bigger”: acting, scaling surprises, explicit reasoning, and data-efficient learning.

🎓 Steven & Div · CS25 guest lecture ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A vanilla LLM call is like asking a brilliant intern to answer from memory, in one breath, without a calculator, browser, notebook, or chance to revise. This lecture asks: what happens when we give the model a notebook, tools, memory, search over possible thoughts, and a smaller-but-better learning diet?

Running analogy — the model as a control room

Keep one image in mind: an LLM is the operator inside a control room. Scaling upgrades the operator’s raw competence; reasoning methods give it a whiteboard; agents connect it to buttons, tools, sensors, and memory; BabyLM asks whether a smaller operator can learn well from a child-sized training library.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before the tour; each term becomes a knob in the control room.

  • LLM & prompting — a transformer language model predicts next tokens from a context; prompts steer which behavior is elicited.
  • Few-shot / in-context learning — examples placed in the prompt can make the model imitate a task without updating weights.
  • Chain-of-thought — prompting for intermediate steps can improve multi-step reasoning because the answer is not forced into one jump.
  • Tool use / agents — an agent is an LLM-driven loop that can choose actions, observe results, and keep working toward a goal.

1.The talk’s map: four ways beyond “bigger LLM”

Start with a concrete failure. Ask a model, “Book the cheapest acceptable flight for my preferences, and explain why.” A one-shot answer is not enough: it needs to search, remember preferences, compare options, maybe ask for approval, and avoid unsafe purchases. The lecture’s four topics are four different upgrades to the control room.

1.1 One system, four frontiers

The first speaker surveys emergent abilities, intermediate-guided reasoning, and BabyLM. Div then turns to agents: systems that wrap the LLM in tools, memory, planning, personalization, and multi-agent coordination.

Key equation — the control-room view

$$ \text{useful system} \approx \text{LLM}_{\theta} + \text{prompting} + \text{memory} + \text{tools} + \text{feedback} + \text{safety rules}. $$

Here \(\text{LLM}_{\theta}\) is the trained model; everything after it is system design. The lecture’s thesis is that future progress will come from both model scale and these surrounding loops.

✓ Check yourself — if the control-room operator gets a web browser but no memory, which lecture topic did you add and which did you still miss?

You added an agent/tool-use component. You still missed persistent memory/personalization: the operator can search the web but cannot reliably remember your long-term preferences.

2.Emergent abilities: the scale surprise

Concrete picture first: a small model guesses nearly randomly on modular arithmetic or word-unscrambling; a much larger model suddenly becomes competent. The 2022 “Emergent Abilities” paper names this pattern: an ability is emergent if it is present in larger models but not smaller ones, and if extrapolating from the smaller models would not have predicted it. For the deeper version, cross-link back to Note 13 on emergent abilities and scaling.

2.1 Phase transition, then caveat: some emergence may be metric-shaped

The lecture emphasizes the phase-transition mental model: performance stays near-random until a critical scale, often discussed around \(10^{22}\) to \(10^{23}\) training FLOPs for some tasks, then jumps. It also flags a modern caveat: a sharp-looking jump can partly come from the metric. If a smooth underlying ability is measured by an all-or-nothing score, the curve can look more discontinuous than the model’s internal competence really is.

Key equation — emergence as a threshold pattern

$$ \text{ability}(N) = \begin{cases} \approx \text{chance}, & N < N_c,\\ \gg \text{chance}, & N \ge N_c, \end{cases} $$

\(N\) is scale, \(N_c\) is a critical threshold, and “chance” is task-dependent. The caveat is that the reported score may be \(g(\text{ability}(N))\), where \(g\) can be a nonlinear metric such as exact match.

Common misconception

“Emergent” does not mean “magic appeared from nowhere.” It means the observed capability was not predictable from the small-model curve. Architecture, data quality, training procedure, prompting, and the evaluation metric all still matter.

✓ Check yourself — why can exact-match scoring make a smooth improvement look like a sudden jump?

If the model moves from “almost right” to “fully right,” exact match gives 0 until the final answer is exact. A continuous improvement in latent reasoning can therefore be recorded as a discontinuous score jump.

3.Intermediate-guided reasoning: give the operator a whiteboard

Suppose a math word problem needs three transformations. A one-shot completion must compress all hidden work into the final token sequence. Intermediate-guided reasoning asks the model to externalize part of that work: steps, scratchpads, code, subproblems, or branches.

3.1 From chain-of-thought to trees, programs, and divide-and-conquer

The lecture starts from chain-of-thought: a sequence of intermediate reasoning steps, useful especially for arithmetic and symbolic tasks. It then broadens the idea: scratchpads are flexible working memory; least-to-most solves simpler subproblems before the full task; tree-of-thoughts searches over branches and can backtrack; program-aided methods offload precise computation to a runtime such as Python.

Key equation — marginalize over reasoning paths

$$ p(y \mid x) = \sum_z p(y, z \mid x) = \sum_z p(y \mid z,x)\,p(z \mid x). $$

\(x\) is the question, \(z\) is an intermediate path, and \(y\) is the final answer. Chain-of-thought samples or constructs \(z\); self-consistency approximates the sum by sampling many \(z\)'s.

Why the whiteboard helps

External steps create more places for useful structure to appear. The model can map symbols, hold partial results, call a calculator, or abandon a bad branch instead of committing to the first fluent answer.

✓ Check yourself — which method is best matched to “try three possible solution strategies, score them, then continue the best one”?

Tree-of-thoughts. It treats reasoning as search over branches rather than a single left-to-right chain.

4.Worked example — self-consistency voting by hand

Now compute the smallest honest version of self-consistency. One question is sampled five times. The sampled final answers are \([42, 42, 7, 42, 7]\).

Step-by-step with real numbers

Counts:

$$ c(42)=3,\qquad c(7)=2,\qquad K=5. $$

The majority-vote estimator is:

$$ \hat y=\arg\max_y \sum_{i=1}^{K}\mathbf{1}[y_i=y] =\arg\max_y c(y)=42. $$

As a probability estimate from sampled paths:

$$ \hat p(42\mid x)=\frac{3}{5}=0.6,\qquad \hat p(7\mid x)=\frac{2}{5}=0.4. $$

A greedy single sample might have returned \(7\) if it followed path 3. Self-consistency spends extra compute to reduce that single-path accident.

✓ Check yourself — what does self-consistency cost, and when is it most worth paying?

It costs roughly \(K\) model generations instead of one, so latency and dollars increase. It helps most when errors vary across reasoning paths and the right answer is more stable than any one sampled chain.

✓ Check yourself — if the samples were [42, 7, 7, 42, 7], what answer wins?

\(7\) wins: \(c(7)=3\), \(c(42)=2\). The estimator follows the majority, not the answer you hoped for.

5.BabyLM: the sample-efficiency frontier

The transcript’s motivating contrast is stark: web-scale models may train on trillions of words, while a child hears only tens of millions. The BabyLM challenge asks whether language models can learn from a developmentally plausible corpus: roughly \(10\)–\(100\) million words, much of it transcribed speech, child-directed language, simple books, subtitles, and some Wikipedia-like text.

5.1 Why small-data pretraining matters

BabyLM is not nostalgia for tiny models. It targets three practical bottlenecks: cognitive plausibility, low-resource accessibility, and greener AI. If we learn better pretraining recipes under severe data limits, the same ideas may transfer upward to larger systems while making research possible outside the largest labs.

Key equation — sample efficiency

$$ \eta = \frac{\text{evaluation score}}{\text{training words}} \qquad\text{or}\qquad \eta_{\Delta} = \frac{\Delta\text{score}}{\Delta\text{words}}. $$

\(\eta\) is not a universal benchmark by itself, but it captures the BabyLM question: how much linguistic competence do we get per word?

Common misconception

BabyLM is not claiming children and transformers learn identically. It is a controlled pressure test: if models need \(10{,}000\) web words for every word a child hears, something about the objective, architecture, data curriculum, or inductive bias is inefficient.

✓ Check yourself — name one scientific and one engineering reason BabyLM matters.

Scientific: it probes how language can be learned from child-scale input. Engineering: data-efficient models reduce cost, energy, and dependence on giant proprietary training runs.

6.LLM agents: connect the control room to the world

Div’s part begins with the agent thesis: humans will communicate with AI in natural language, while AI operates machines and software. A single model call is a component; an agent is a system that chains calls, uses tools, reads observations, stores memory, plans, and acts. This connects naturally to Note 20 on generalist agents in open-ended worlds.

6.1 ReAct, tools, memory, autonomy, and multi-agent systems

The cleanest loop is ReAct: interleave reasoning and acting. The agent writes a Thought, chooses an Action such as search, calculator, browser click, code interpreter, or API call, receives an Observation, then repeats until it can answer or complete the task.

Key equation — an agent as a policy over actions

$$ a_t \sim \pi_{\theta}\!\left(a \mid q,\; h_t,\; m_t,\; \mathcal{T}\right), \qquad h_{t+1}=h_t \oplus (a_t,o_t). $$

\(q\) is the user goal, \(h_t\) is the current trajectory, \(m_t\) is memory, \(\mathcal{T}\) is the available tool set, \(a_t\) is the chosen action, \(o_t\) is the observation, and \(\oplus\) means “append to the working history.”

6.2 What gets added around the LLM

  • Tools / function calling — calculators, calendars, search, code interpreters, browser controllers, APIs, or computer-use actions like clicking and typing.
  • Planning — decomposing a task into subgoals, often with chain-of-thought, tree-of-thoughts, or manager-worker hierarchies.
  • Memory — short-term context plus long-term storage retrieved by embeddings or structured indexes.
  • Personalization — user-agent alignment: learning preferences explicitly from instructions and implicitly from behavior.
  • Multi-agent coordination — specialized workers, parallel execution, verification agents, communication protocols, and managers.
  • Safety and observability — permissions, sandboxing, kill switches, logs, benchmarks, and feedback loops to prevent divergence.
Common misconception

“Agent” does not automatically mean “reliable autonomous employee.” The transcript repeatedly points to hard unsolved problems: looping, stochastic failure, prompt injection, privacy, permissions, benchmarking, and deployment monitoring.

✓ Check yourself — why might direct browser control be more general but riskier than API calls?

Browser control can operate any website like a human, even without a formal API. But the action space is messier: clicks can misfire, pages can contain prompt injections, and irreversible actions may require stronger permissions and human approval.

7.End-to-end trace — one question through a ReAct agent

Follow one concrete multi-hop factual question through the loop: “Which is farther north: the capital of Canada or the capital of Australia?”

StepWhat happensOutput
0 · questionUser asks the comparison question.Need two capitals + latitudes
1 · ThoughtAgent plans to identify each country’s capital, then compare latitude.Plan: Canada → capital; Australia → capital; compare
2 · ActionAgent calls search/tool: “capital of Canada latitude.”Search query issued
3 · ObservationTool returns Ottawa and latitude about \(45.4^\circ\) N.Ottawa ≈ 45.4° N
4 · ThoughtAgent records first result and needs the second capital.Need Canberra latitude
5 · ActionAgent calls search/tool: “capital of Australia latitude.”Search query issued
6 · ObservationTool returns Canberra and latitude about \(35.3^\circ\) S.Canberra ≈ 35.3° S
7 · ThoughtAgent compares signed latitudes: north is positive, south is negative.45.4 > -35.3
8 · Final answerAgent answers with evidence and minimal explanation.Ottawa is farther north than Canberra.
What the trace reveals

Externalizing reasoning/actions beats one-shot answering because each step creates a checkpoint. The agent can fetch facts instead of guessing them, store observations, and compare explicit state before committing to the final answer.

✓ Check yourself — where would a verification agent fit in this trace?

After steps 3 and 6, a verifier could independently check the retrieved capitals/latitudes. After step 7, it could verify the sign convention before the final answer is returned.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
AgentAn LLM-centered system that can choose actions, use tools, observe results, and continue working toward a goal.
ReActA prompting/control pattern that alternates reasoning traces with external actions and observations.
Function callingLetting a model select a structured tool/API call instead of only emitting free-form text.
Emergent abilityA capability observed in larger models but absent or unpredictable from smaller-model scaling curves.
Metric mirageA sharp capability jump caused or exaggerated by the evaluation metric rather than a truly discontinuous internal change.
Chain-of-thoughtIntermediate natural-language reasoning steps before the final answer.
ScratchpadExternal working space where a model writes partial computations, facts, or plans.
Self-consistencySampling multiple reasoning paths and majority-voting the final answer.
Least-to-mostSolving simpler subproblems first, then using them to solve the full problem.
Tree-of-thoughtsSearch over multiple reasoning branches, with scoring and possible backtracking.
BabyLMA challenge focused on training language models with developmentally plausible, small-scale corpora.
User-agent alignmentMaking an agent’s actions match a particular user’s preferences, permissions, and safety expectations.
