Emergent Abilities & Scaling in Large Language Models

Scaling buys two different things: predictable loss reductions you can forecast, and task abilities that can look absent until a large model crosses a threshold.

🎓 Jason Wei / CS25 guest ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Suppose you are deciding whether to spend millions training a larger model. If all you get is a slightly lower next-token loss, the business and safety case is one kind of argument. If scale suddenly unlocks multi-step arithmetic, exam performance, instruction following, or new failure modes, the argument changes. This lecture separates the smooth part of scaling from the surprising part.

Running analogy — climbing a mountain with two instruments

Think of model scale as altitude. A barometer changes smoothly as you climb: that is language-model loss. A checkpoint gate stays closed until you reach a pass, then opens: that is an exact-match benchmark. The mountain is real, but what looks like a cliff can depend on which instrument you read.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

You'll follow the note smoothly if these are fresh.

  • Language-model loss / perplexity — cross-entropy measures how surprised the model is by held-out text; perplexity is its exponentiated form, so lower means better next-token prediction.
  • Power law / log-log plot — a relationship like error \(\propto\) size-α becomes close to a straight line when both axes are logarithmic; this is why scaling-law plots look so clean.
  • Few-shot prompting — give a pretrained model a few input-output examples in the context window, then ask it to continue the pattern on a new example.
  • Accuracy vs cross-entropy — accuracy is often all-or-nothing after decoding; cross-entropy gives partial credit to better probabilities even before the final answer flips.

1.What scale buys: two faces of the same climb

In the transcript, Wei begins with the familiar result: as compute, parameters, or data increase, test loss goes down predictably. Then he pivots: some downstream abilities look impossible to predict from small models because small-model accuracy is flat near random until larger models appear.

1.1 A smooth gauge and a threshold gauge

The smooth gauge asks, “Did the model assign better probabilities to the next tokens?” The threshold gauge asks, “Did the final answer pass the benchmark?” Those can tell different stories about the same underlying improvement.

Definition with epistemic status

Working definition from Wei et al.: an ability is emergent if it is not present in smaller models but is present in larger models. This is an empirical benchmark definition, not a proof that the internal computation changed discontinuously.

✓ Check yourself — which instrument would notice progress first?

If a model becomes less wrong token by token but still fails exact answer matching, cross-entropy or per-token accuracy notices first. Exact-match accuracy may remain flat until every required part is correct.

2.Scaling laws: the predictable part

Before talking about surprise, anchor on the boring miracle: for many orders of magnitude, bigger language models trained on more data with more compute become predictably less surprised by held-out text.

2.1 Kaplan-style power laws

Kaplan et al. showed that test loss is well approximated by simple power laws in model size, dataset size, and compute. If you only watch this gauge, scaling looks almost engineered: spend more compute, get a forecastable reduction in loss.

Key equation — loss vs model size

$$L(N) \approx L_\infty + \left(\frac{N_0}{N}\right)^\alpha$$

\(L(N)\) is held-out loss for a model with \(N\) parameters; \(L_\infty\) is the irreducible floor for this data distribution and architecture family; \(N_0\) sets the scale; \(\alpha>0\) controls how quickly returns diminish.

2.2 Chinchilla: many large models were undertrained

Hoffmann et al. refined the story: for a fixed compute budget, do not spend nearly all of it on parameters. Scale model size \(N\) and training tokens \(D\) roughly together. The lecture notes that Chinchilla was unusual in the plotted families because larger Chinchilla models used more data, not only more parameters.

Key equation — training compute budget

$$C \approx 6ND$$

For dense decoder-only transformers, training compute \(C\) is roughly proportional to parameter count \(N\) times token count \(D\). Chinchilla's practical message: for compute-optimal training, increase both instead of building a huge model that sees too few tokens.

Common misconception

“Scaling” does not mean “increase parameters only.” In the transcript, Wei lists three scale axes: training FLOPs, parameter count, and dataset size. Better data, more in-domain examples, architecture, and fine-tuning can move an apparent threshold.

✓ Check yourself — if compute quadruples, what does “scale N and D equally” suggest?

Because \(C\propto ND\), multiplying \(N\) by 2 and \(D\) by 2 multiplies compute by 4. That is the rough Chinchilla-style direction, ignoring hardware and architecture details.

3.Emergent abilities: the surprising part

Now switch instruments. On many few-shot tasks, small models perform around random. Then a much larger model crosses a threshold and performance rises sharply. If you extrapolated only from the small points, you would not forecast the jump.

3.1 What jumped in the lecture

  • MMLU / multitask NLU: professional and school exam questions; models near \(10^{22}\) training FLOPs look close to random, while larger models near \(10^{24}\) FLOPs improve substantially.
  • BIG-bench IPA transliteration: convert English sentences to International Phonetic Alphabet-style pronunciations; performance can stay flat and then improve.
  • “Inverse scaling” style examples: “Repeat this sentence” can get worse at medium scale when the model knows the famous phrase but ignores the instruction; larger models can recover by understanding both the phrase and the command.
  • Prompting and alignment techniques: RLHF and chain-of-thought can help only after the model is capable enough for the technique to be useful.

3.2 Thresholds are not laws of nature

Wei emphasizes that a 100B-parameter-looking threshold is not sacred. PaLM appeared to unlock some BIG-bench abilities at smaller parameter counts than LaMDA or GPT-3, plausibly because of better data and training choices. Once a desired behavior is identified, distillation or fine-tuning can induce it in smaller models.

Interpretation

Emergence is useful as a map of observed capabilities: “below this region we have not seen the ability; above it we have.” It is weaker as a mechanistic claim unless we rule out metric artifacts, data effects, and prompt effects.

✓ Check yourself — why can a medium model be worse than a small model on “repeat the sentence”?

The small model may copy literally because it does not know the famous phrase. The medium model may know the phrase and “correct” glue to gold, violating the instruction. A larger model can know the phrase and obey the repeat command.

4.The mirage critique: when metrics manufacture cliffs

The strongest counter-argument is not “models do not improve.” It is sharper: some reported emergence may be an artifact of how we score outputs. Schaeffer et al. argue that discontinuous or nonlinear metrics, especially exact-match accuracy, can turn smooth underlying improvement into an apparent jump.

4.1 The honest two-sided view

QuestionEmergence viewMirage view
What is real?Large models perform qualitatively new tasks above a scale threshold.Underlying competence often improves smoothly; the scoring rule reveals it late.
Best evidenceMMLU, BIG-bench, arithmetic, word unscrambling, prompting techniques that work only at scale.Re-plotting with continuous metrics like token-level loss or edit distance can remove sharp jumps.
Practical takeawayDo not assume small-model curves bound large-model capability.Do not trust one harsh metric to describe capability acquisition.
Key equation — exact match compounds token difficulty

$$P(\text{all }k\text{ tokens correct}) = p^k$$

If each required token is correct with probability \(p\), a harsh exact-match metric for \(k\) independent required tokens scales as \(p^k\). Smooth changes in \(p\) become much sharper after exponentiation.

Common misconception

“Mirage” does not mean “nothing was learned.” It means the shape of the benchmark curve may exaggerate discontinuity. A model can genuinely become better while the cliff-like graph is partly a measurement artifact.

✓ Check yourself — name one metric that would show partial progress on word unscrambling.

Edit distance, character-level accuracy, per-token log probability, or partial-credit accuracy would show whether the model is getting closer even when exact match remains zero.

5.Worked example — how a metric manufactures emergence

Use the smallest honest arithmetic version of the mirage argument. The underlying capability is smooth: per-token accuracy rises from 0.30 to 0.60 to 0.90 across three model sizes. The benchmark, however, demands a 4-token answer with every token correct.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Let \(p\) be per-token accuracy and let exact match require \(k=4\) correct tokens:

$$\text{ExactMatch}(p)=p^4$$

Small model: $$0.30^4 = 0.30\times0.30\times0.30\times0.30 = 0.0081 \approx 0.008$$

Medium model: $$0.60^4 = 0.60\times0.60\times0.60\times0.60 = 0.1296 \approx 0.13$$

Large model: $$0.90^4 = 0.90\times0.90\times0.90\times0.90 = 0.6561 \approx 0.66$$

The per-token metric moved smoothly: \(0.30\to0.60\to0.90\). The exact-match metric looks like a dramatic jump: \(0.008\to0.13\to0.66\).

✓ Check yourself — redo it for a 2-token task at p = 0.30, 0.60, 0.90

For \(k=2\): \(0.30^2=0.09\), \(0.60^2=0.36\), and \(0.90^2=0.81\). The curve still bends upward, but it looks less cliff-like than the 4-token exact-match task.

The same run can support two true statements: “the model improved smoothly at the subanswer level” and “the benchmark success appeared suddenly.” Good evaluation reports both instruments.

7.Chain-of-thought prompting: emergence you can elicit

Wei's second major topic is chain-of-thought prompting. Instead of showing only question-answer pairs, you show intermediate reasoning steps: “like your teacher asking you to show your work.” This helps large models solve tasks where one-shot answer generation is too compressed.

7.1 Why it helps at scale

For arithmetic, navigation, word sorting, and StrategyQA-style commonsense reasoning, a model may need to maintain intermediate state. Chain of thought gives the model a token-by-token workspace. But it is also scale-sensitive: small models may ramble, repeat, or fail to produce coherent steps.

Key equation — self-consistency voting

$$\hat{a}=\arg\max_a \sum_{i=1}^{m} \mathbf{1}[a_i=a]$$

Sample \(m\) reasoning paths at nonzero temperature, extract final answers \(a_i\), then choose the majority answer \(\hat{a}\). The lecture reports large gains on GSM8K-style math because diverse paths make independent mistakes.

✓ Check yourself — why does “xxxxx” padding not explain chain-of-thought gains?

The transcript notes that length-matched nonsense prompts do not work. The extra tokens help because they encode useful intermediate state and decomposition, not merely because the model receives more inference compute.

8.Implications: forecasting, evaluation, and safety

The practical lesson is not “bigger is magic.” It is: when scale changes both probability quality and task-level behavior, we need better forecasts and better measurements.

8.1 What to do differently

  • Forecast with smooth metrics such as loss, perplexity, calibration, and partial credit; they give earlier signal.
  • Evaluate with harsh metrics too when the application really is all-or-nothing, such as code passing tests or exact database queries.
  • Do not extrapolate only from small models for high-stakes capabilities; the lecture frames emergence as a reason people kept scaling.
  • Search for undesirable thresholds: harmful or deceptive capabilities could also appear late, so safety evaluations should scale with model capability.
  • Use targeted data and distillation once you know the desired behavior; production systems often prefer smaller, cheaper specialized models.
Epistemic status — where I would be cautious

High confidence: loss scaling is smooth over broad ranges; exact-match metrics can amplify smooth progress into jumps. Medium confidence: some abilities remain genuinely hard to predict from small-model curves even under better metrics. Open question: which future capabilities can be forecast before training frontier-scale models?

✓ Check yourself — design a better emergence plot for one task

Plot at least two y-axes or panels: exact-match accuracy and a continuous proxy such as edit distance, per-token loss, or partial-credit score. Keep the same x-axis across model families and mark chance performance.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Scaling lawA predictable mathematical relationship between model/data/compute scale and loss, often a power law.
Emergent abilityA benchmark ability absent or random in smaller models but present in larger models.
Training FLOPsTotal floating-point operations spent during training; a proxy for scale.
PerplexityExponentiated cross-entropy loss; roughly, the model's average uncertainty about the next token.
Exact matchA harsh metric that gives credit only when the full predicted answer equals the target.
BIG-benchA broad benchmark suite of diverse tasks for language models.
MMLUMassive Multitask Language Understanding, a benchmark of exam-style questions across many subjects.
Chain-of-thought promptingPrompting a model to produce intermediate reasoning steps before the final answer.
Self-consistencySampling several reasoning paths and taking a majority vote over their final answers.
Compute-optimal trainingChoosing model size and training tokens to minimize loss for a fixed compute budget.
