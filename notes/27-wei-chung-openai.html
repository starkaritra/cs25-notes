Intuitions on Language Models & Scaling — Jason Wei & Hyung Won Chung

A practitioner note on why next-token prediction can become many skills, and why the winning structure in AI tends to get simpler as compute grows.

🎓 Jason Wei & Hyung Won Chung (OpenAI) ⏱ ~1 hr ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Two OpenAI practitioners are not trying to prove a theorem here. They are teaching a way to think. Jason Wei asks: what does the next-token loss force a model to learn? Hyung Won Chung asks: what assumptions should researchers remove once scale makes them unnecessary? If you can hold both questions at once, modern LLM behavior becomes less magical and more inspectable.

Running analogy — the giant training gym

Picture pretraining as a giant gym. Every sentence is a station: one asks for grammar, another facts, another translation, another arithmetic. Scaling buys more floor space, more equipment, and a stronger trainee. Hyung Won's point is that early handrails help beginners, but later they can block the strongest athletes; the research job is to remove handrails when the gym gets large enough.

Epistemic status

This note marks the talk as intuition and perspective. Scaling laws and cross-entropy are measurable facts; claims like "this structure is now a bottleneck" are practitioner judgments, useful precisely because they expose the assumptions behind research taste.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before the argument; each term is used as a building block, not as the main lesson.

  • Language-model loss / cross-entropy — the model assigns a probability to the actual next token; lower loss means it was less surprised. Jason's whole argument starts here.
  • Scaling laws — loss often improves predictably as parameters, data, and compute grow; see Note 13 for the deeper scaling/emergence treatment.
  • Inductive bias — structure we impose to make learning easier, such as bidirectional encoders or separate source/target modules. Hyung Won asks when those biases become bottlenecks.
  • Encoder-decoder vs decoder-only — encoder-decoder separates input processing from output generation; decoder-only concatenates input and target into one causal sequence.

1.Jason Wei: next-token prediction is secretly a huge curriculum

Jason starts from a deceptively plain task. Given Dartmouth students like to, the model assigns probabilities to every vocabulary word and is trained to put high probability on the observed next word. His advice is unusually concrete: manually inspect the data. He tells a lung-cancer-classification story to make the methodological point: if you stare at real examples until you can almost do the task yourself, you get intuitions that abstract metrics hide.

1.1 Inspect the data: what is each token asking?

Take the Wikipedia-style fragment from the talk: Biden married Neilia Hunter, a student... Predicting Hunter asks for world knowledge; predicting the comma asks for punctuation; predicting a asks for grammar; predicting student is a more arbitrary blend of context and biography. The "task" is not one clean benchmark. The gym has millions of tiny stations.

Key equation — next-token loss

$$\mathcal{L}_t=-\log P_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})$$

At position \(t\), \(x_t\) is the actual next token, \(x_{<t}\) is the preceding context, and \(P_\theta\) is the model's probability distribution over the vocabulary. Training pushes the probability of the observed token toward 1.

1.2 One objective, many skills

Jason's core intuition is massively multi-task learning. Grammar, lexical semantics, world knowledge, sentiment, translation, spatial reasoning, and arithmetic all appear as next-token predictions somewhere in the corpus. The model is not explicitly told "now do translation"; it is rewarded for predicting the continuation that translation-like contexts demand.

Common misconception

"It only learns surface statistics." Sometimes that is enough; sometimes the cheapest way to lower next-token loss is to store a fact, infer a location, follow an instruction, or compute. Jason's claim is not that every prediction is deep reasoning; it is that the loss does not care which internal method lowers surprise.

✓ Check yourself — classify the hidden task

For "The word for beautiful in Spanish is ___", the local hidden task is translation plus lexical semantics. The model receives the same cross-entropy signal as everywhere else; only the context makes the station translation-shaped.

2.Worked micro-example — next-token prediction forces multi-task learning

Now compute the smallest honest case. Context: The capital of France is ___ . The target token is Paris. A tiny vocabulary slice has four candidates.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Suppose the model assigns logits

$$z=[z_{\text{Paris}},z_{\text{London}},z_{\text{banana}},z_{\text{is}}]=[3,1,0,-1].$$

The softmax probability of Paris is

$$ P(\text{Paris}\mid x_{<t})= \frac{e^3}{e^3+e^1+e^0+e^{-1}} =\frac{20.085}{20.085+2.718+1+0.368}\approx0.831. $$

Cross-entropy for the observed target is

$$\mathcal{L}=-\log P(x_t\mid x_{<t})=-\log(0.831)\approx0.185.$$

If the corpus instead contains The word for pretty in Spanish is ___, the same formula rewards bonita. If it contains 7 + 8 = ___, the same formula rewards 15. One objective, many skills.

Context stationCorrect next tokenSkill pressured by the same loss
"The capital of France is"ParisWorld knowledge
"The word for pretty in Spanish is"bonitaTranslation / lexical semantics
"7 + 8 ="15Arithmetic pattern or computation
"Iroh went to the kitchen... Zuko left the"kitchenSpatial and discourse tracking
✓ Check yourself — why does this scale to many abilities?

Because every token in a diverse corpus is a supervised example. As data variety grows, the same loss supplies gradients for facts, syntax, translation, arithmetic, instruction following, and many arbitrary mixtures. The objective is narrow; the contexts are not.

3.Jason Wei: why scaling can look smooth and sudden at the same time

Jason then separates two observations. First, scaling compute — roughly more data times a larger model — reliably improves overall loss. Second, individual abilities can appear suddenly because the overall loss is an average over many hidden tasks, some already saturated and some still far from solved.

3.1 Smooth total loss, sharp local abilities

A large model can store more tail facts and execute more complex functions in its forward pass. In Jason's terms, memorization and reasoning are not separate training objectives; both are ways to win the same next-word game. A small model may learn first-order heuristics because that is enough to reduce loss; a larger model has room to memorize rarer facts and represent more complex procedures.

Key equation — total loss as a mixture of hidden tasks

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{overall}} \approx w_g\mathcal{L}_{\text{grammar}} +w_f\mathcal{L}_{\text{facts}} +w_m\mathcal{L}_{\text{math}} +w_r\mathcal{L}_{\text{reasoning}}+\cdots $$

The weights \(w_i\) reflect how often those token situations appear. Grammar may saturate early; math or instruction following may improve much later. A smooth average can hide a delayed jump in one component.

He also discusses inverse or U-shaped scaling with the prompt "Repeat after me. All that glisters is not glib, all that glisters is not ___". A tiny model may repeat glib; a middle model knows the Shakespeare quote and incorrectly "fixes" it to gold; a larger model can both know the quote and follow the instruction, so it returns glib. The curve is weird because multiple hidden subtasks conflict.

3.2 Chain-of-thought as giving the gym a scratchpad

Chain-of-thought prompting asks the model to produce intermediate reasoning tokens before the final answer. Jason's broader intuition makes this less mysterious: if reasoning traces exist in the data, then predicting them is another next-token station. At scale, the model can use generated tokens as a working scratchpad, turning a single hard jump into a sequence of easier next-token steps.

Connection to Note 13

For a deeper treatment of emergent abilities, metrics, and the "mirage" critique, see Note 13. Jason's stance in the Q&A is practical: metrics can change the visual shape, but the useful capabilities themselves are real enough to study.

✓ Check yourself — explain the U-shape without saying "the model got worse"

The middle model gained one subskill: quote correction. But it had not yet gained the stronger instruction-following subskill. The aggregate answer got worse because the newly learned subskill conflicted with the prompt's instruction.

4.End-to-end trace — the bitter lesson in action

Hyung Won's half begins by studying change itself. If many forces act, prediction is hard; if one dominant force acts, you can get directionality. His proposed dominant force for AI is the long-term fall in compute cost and the scaling efforts it enables. Follow that force across eras.

StepEraWhat structure was added / removedResult
0Hand-engineered MLadd human-designed features, rules, task-specific pipelinesWorks in low-compute regimes, but the handrails encode human assumptions and cap flexibility.
1CNNs / RNNsadd useful inductive biases: locality, recurrence, sequence orderHuge gains when data and compute are limited; still more general than hand features.
2Transformersremove much recurrence; use attention and scalable parallel trainingLess hand-coded sequence machinery; more compute and data can be exploited.
3Encoder-decoder / encoder-only periodadd source-target separation, bidirectional encoders, task-specific headsStrong for translation, SQuAD, GLUE, and academic fine-tuning where those assumptions fit.
4Decoder-only at scaleremove separate input/target stacks; use one causal sequence modelOne simple interface handles pretraining, prompting, chat turns, tool text, and long generation.
The bitter lesson, in this talk's language

General methods that leverage computation tend to win over methods that encode our hand-built understanding. But the lesson is not "never add structure." It is: add the minimum structure that works at today's compute, remember that it was a shortcut, and remove it when scale makes it a bottleneck.

✓ Check yourself — what makes the trace "bitter"?

The bitter part is that clever human structure often helps now and hurts later. The work that looks sophisticated in a low-compute regime can become the thing preventing the next scale-up from using data and compute freely.

5.Hyung Won Chung: the right assumptions change as compute grows

Hyung Won compares transformer variants not mainly to teach architecture, but to show how historical assumptions become negotiable. Encoder-decoder was natural when machine translation was the flagship task. Decoder-only becomes natural when the goal is a general text interface trained and used as one long sequence.

5.1 From encoder-decoder to decoder-only

In an encoder-decoder transformer, the input is encoded bidirectionally, the output is generated causally, and cross-attention connects decoder layers to the final encoder states. In a decoder-only transformer, the input and target are concatenated; causal self-attention serves both "read the prompt" and "generate the answer" roles.

Key equation — causal factorization

$$P(x_1,\ldots,x_T)=\prod_{t=1}^{T}P(x_t\mid x_{<t})$$

A decoder-only model treats prompt tokens, answer tokens, previous chat turns, and sometimes tool text as one sequence. The same factorization supports pretraining and generation.

5.2 Why the old assumptions became less attractive

Hyung Won names several structures in encoder-decoder systems and asks whether they still match the modern regime:

  • Separate input/target parameters. Useful when English input and German output are genuinely different. Less natural when knowledge appears across languages and when this turn's answer becomes next turn's input.
  • Decoder attends to final encoder layer. Perhaps fine at 24 layers, but a possible information bottleneck if models become dramatically deeper.
  • Bidirectional input attention. Very useful for BERT-era SQuAD/GLUE, but less decisive at scale and awkward for chat serving because new turns can force re-encoding. Causal attention supports key-value caching.
  • Encoder-only classifiers. Productive for classification benchmarks, but giving up generation was a simplifying structure that did not become the most general interface.
Common misconception

"Less structure means worse science." Hyung Won's point is subtler: structure is a loan from the future. It buys progress when compute is scarce, but the interest is paid when the structure stops matching the scaled-up task.

✓ Check yourself — why is multi-turn chat awkward for bidirectional encoders?

If earlier tokens can attend to later tokens, adding a new assistant or user turn can change representations of previous tokens, so you may need to encode old context again. In a causal decoder, previous key/value states remain valid because past tokens never attend to future tokens.

6.Don't teach, incentivize

Hyung Won frames the AI researcher's job as teaching machines how to think, then warns against teaching them how we think we think. We do not understand cognition well enough to hand-code it. A more scalable strategy is to specify incentives that reward general capability and let learning discover the mechanism.

Practitioner slogan

Don't teach, incentivize. Instead of hand-writing narrow behaviors, choose objectives, data, feedback, and evaluation pressures that reward broad competence. Next-token prediction is one crude incentive; instruction tuning and RLHF are attempts to shape behavior with broader feedback than a single hand-coded rule.

This also explains his discomfort with maximum-likelihood training for open-ended chat. In supervised learning, one target string is treated as the only correct continuation. For a factual label this may be fine; for "write a poem" it is a harsh, over-structured teaching signal. A learned reward model in RLHF is not presented as the final answer, but as evidence that learned objectives can remove some of that rigid structure.

Key equation — from one target to a learned preference signal

$$\text{MLE: }\max_\theta \log P_\theta(y^\star\mid x) \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \text{RLHF-style: }\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{y\sim P_\theta(\cdot\mid x)}[R_\phi(x,y)]$$

In MLE, \(y^\star\) is the single recorded target. In the reward formulation, \(R_\phi(x,y)\) is a learned scoring function over possible outputs. The latter can represent "many good poems" more naturally, though it introduces its own scalability and reward-modeling problems.

What researchers should do with this

Jason's prescription is to plot scaling curves and inspect data. Hyung Won's prescription is to ask, for every added structure: What assumption does this encode? Was it only helpful because compute was scarce? Can a more general method now absorb it? The two prescriptions meet: measure the curve, then decide which handrail to remove.

✓ Check yourself — what would be a "teaching" solution vs an "incentivizing" solution?

Teaching: write a rule that all summaries must follow a fixed template. Incentivizing: train or evaluate with feedback that rewards faithful, concise, useful summaries across many formats, allowing the model to learn when the template helps and when it hurts.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Next-token predictionTraining a model to predict the next token from the preceding context.
Cross-entropy lossThe penalty \(-\log p\) for assigning probability \(p\) to the observed token.
Massively multi-task learningLearning many implicit skills because diverse contexts all use the same predictive objective.
Scaling lawAn empirical relationship where loss changes predictably with compute, data, or model size.
Emergent abilityA benchmark ability that is absent or near-random in smaller models and appears in larger ones.
Chain-of-thoughtPrompting or training a model to produce intermediate reasoning tokens before the final answer.
Inductive biasA structural assumption that makes some functions easier for a model to learn than others.
Bitter lessonRich Sutton's observation that general methods leveraging computation tend to outperform hand-engineered domain structure over time.
Encoder-decoderA transformer layout with a separate input encoder and output decoder connected by cross-attention.
Decoder-onlyA causal transformer that models one sequence and predicts each token from previous tokens.
Bidirectional attentionAn attention mask where tokens can attend to both earlier and later input tokens.
RLHFReinforcement learning from human feedback; a family of methods that train models using learned preference or reward signals.
