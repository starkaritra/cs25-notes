Language & Human Alignment

Jan Leike frames alignment as recruiting increasingly capable AI systems onto Team Human: pretraining gives a language model abilities, but human feedback is what teaches it to use those abilities in ways people actually intend.

🎓 Jan Leike ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A pretrained language model has learned an astonishing skill: continue text that looks likely on the internet. But a user does not ask, “what text usually comes next?” The user asks, “help me, tell the truth, avoid harm, and ask when you are unsure.” Those are different targets.

Leike’s lecture starts from a strategic picture: Team AI keeps sending stronger players into the game. Team Human’s special advantage is that, for now, we choose which AI players are deployed and how they are trained. Alignment is the attempt to recruit those players to play on Team Human.

Running analogy — recruiting a star player

Imagine a brilliant new athlete joining your team. Raw talent is pretraining: speed, memory, language, tactics. Alignment is coaching: learning the playbook, listening to the captain, not fouling, and knowing when to pass instead of showing off. A bigger athlete is not automatically a better teammate.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

These are the tiny tools the lecture assumes; keep them active as we build the pipeline.

  • Language modeling objective — pretraining minimizes next-token loss, e.g. \(-\sum_t \log p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})\); this teaches prediction, not obedience or values.
  • Policy and reward in RL — a policy \(\pi(y\mid x)\) chooses an output \(y\) for prompt \(x\), while a reward \(r(x,y)\) scores how desirable that output is.
  • Logistic sigmoid — \(\sigma(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})\) turns a score difference into a probability; we use it for pairwise preferences.
  • KL divergence — \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)\) measures how far one distribution has drifted from another; RLHF uses it as a leash to the supervised model.
  • Fine-tuning — continue training a pretrained model on a smaller targeted dataset; here, demonstrations and preferences steer behavior without redoing pretraining.

1.The alignment gap: prediction is not intent-following

Start with a plain prompt: “Help me write a safe chemistry study plan.” A raw pretrained model may produce a plausible web-like continuation. That continuation might be verbose, false, unsafe, or oddly formatted. The issue is not lack of language ability; it is that the training target did not say “be a helpful assistant.”

1.1 Explicit and implicit intent

Leike separates two things users want. Explicit intent is the instruction on the page: summarize this, solve that, answer in Spanish. Implicit intent is the unstated contract: do not invent citations, do not enable harm, ask clarifying questions, respect the user’s actual meaning rather than the most literal reading.

Common misconception

“A bigger LM automatically wants to help you.” Bigger pretraining can produce more knowledge and more latent capability, but the objective remains text prediction. Leike’s InstructGPT example is the counterpunch: a much smaller instruction-tuned model was preferred by humans over a much larger GPT-3 model because it was actually trying to follow the user’s intent.

Key equation — pretraining target versus human target

$$ \underbrace{\min_\theta\; \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\mathcal{D}_{\text{text}}}[-\log p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})]}_{\text{predict likely text}} \quad \neq \quad \underbrace{\max_\pi\; \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\mathcal{D}_{\text{users}},\;y\sim\pi(\cdot\mid x)}[U_{\text{human}}(x,y)]}_{\text{satisfy human intent}} $$

The left side rewards matching a text distribution. The right side asks for utility to humans: helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and context-sensitive preference.

✓ Check yourself — why can a more capable model be less useful?

Because capability and objective are different. A model can know many facts yet still optimize for a continuation style that is evasive, fabricated, unsafe, or unresponsive to the user’s instruction.

2.RLHF: the canonical three-stage recipe

The lecture describes the core trick behind InstructGPT and early ChatGPT: instead of writing every value as a rule, collect human judgments about model behavior, train a model of those judgments, and then optimize the assistant against that learned reward.

2.1 Stage 1 and 2: demonstrations, then comparisons

First, humans write demonstrations of good assistant behavior. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) turns the pretrained model into a first-pass assistant. Then the SFT model and nearby variants generate several answers to the same prompt; human labelers rank or compare them. This is easier than writing the perfect answer from scratch, which becomes a major theme of the talk.

Key equation — supervised fine-tuning

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SFT}}(\theta)=-\mathbb{E}_{(x,y^*)\sim\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{demo}}}\log \pi_\theta(y^*\mid x) $$

Here \(x\) is a prompt, \(y^*\) is a human demonstration, and \(\pi_\theta\) is the assistant policy being trained to imitate the demonstrated response.

Key equation — Bradley–Terry reward-model loss

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RM}} = -\log \sigma\big(r_\phi(x,y_w)-r_\phi(x,y_l)\big) $$

The reward model \(r_\phi\) assigns a scalar score to a prompt-response pair. The human-preferred response is \(y_w\); the less-preferred response is \(y_l\). The loss is small when the preferred answer receives the higher score.

2.2 Stage 3: optimize, but keep the model on a leash

Once the reward model exists, RL can search for responses that score well. OpenAI used PPO in InstructGPT; Leike notes PPO was not sacred, but it worked. The critical guardrail is a KL penalty to the SFT model, which discourages the policy from drifting into strange reward-exploiting language.

Key equation — RLHF objective with KL penalty

$$ \max_\pi\; \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\mathcal{D},\;y\sim\pi(\cdot\mid x)}\left[ r_\phi(x,y) - \beta\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\pi(\cdot\mid x)\,\|\,\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}(\cdot\mid x)\right) \right] $$

The first term says “get high human-preference reward.” The second says “do not move too far from the supervised assistant.” \(\beta\) controls the leash strength.

✓ Check yourself — why not ask humans to label every PPO sample directly?

You could, but it is data-inefficient and slow. The reward model amortizes human judgment: once trained, it can score many candidate outputs, while humans only provide a smaller set of comparisons.

3.What worked in InstructGPT and ChatGPT

Leike emphasizes how surprising the first result felt: human preference curves showed even the largest GPT-3 model being dispreferred to the smallest InstructGPT variant. Alignment tuning did not add most capabilities; it unlocked capabilities already latent in pretraining by making the model try to help.

3.1 Evaluation is easier than generation

Why can preference data work so well? Because judging two answers is often easier than writing the better answer yourself. You may not be a professional e-sports player, phone designer, or paper author, but you can often tell who is winning, which phone you prefer, or what is wrong with a draft.

The lecture’s central lever

If humans can reliably evaluate outputs slightly above their own generation ability, then models can learn behavior that exceeds what the labeler could personally produce. RLHF depends on that gap. Scalable oversight tries to widen it with AI assistance.

Subtle limitation

SFT imitates humans. That can be good when humans are better than the model, but it can also constrain the model to human ways of doing a task. RLHF lets the model find its own way to satisfy the preference signal. The danger is that it may also find unintended ways to satisfy an imperfect signal.

ChatGPT, in Leike’s telling, improved the interface: dialogue lets users ask follow-up questions, request refinements, and steer the assistant more naturally. It also got better at refusing harmful tasks. But he names two persistent signs of misalignment: hallucination and sensitivity to prompt phrasing.

✓ Check yourself — what does hallucination reveal about the objective?

It reveals that sounding plausible and being true are not the same objective. A model can be fluent and helpful-looking while fabricating facts, so honesty has to be trained, evaluated, and stress-tested separately.

4.Worked micro-example — one reward-model comparison

Take one prompt and two candidate answers. A human prefers answer \(y_w\) over answer \(y_l\). The reward model currently scores them as \(r_w=1.8\) and \(r_l=0.9\).

Step-by-step with real numbers

Compute the score gap:

$$ \Delta = r_w-r_l = 1.8-0.9=0.9 $$

Turn that gap into a Bradley–Terry preference probability:

$$ P(y_w\succ y_l)=\sigma(0.9)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-0.9}}\approx\frac{1}{1+0.4066}\approx0.7109 $$

Now compute the loss:

$$ \mathcal{L}=-\log(0.7109)\approx0.341 $$

Gradient-sign intuition: \(\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\Delta}=\sigma(\Delta)-1\approx-0.289\). Gradient descent therefore increases \(\Delta\): push \(r_w\) up relative to \(r_l\), or push \(r_l\) down relative to \(r_w\).

✓ Check yourself — what if the two scores were equal?

If \(r_w=r_l\), then \(\Delta=0\), \(\sigma(0)=0.5\), and \(\mathcal{L}=-\log 0.5=\log 2\approx0.693\). The gradient with respect to \(\Delta\) is \(-0.5\), so training strongly pushes the preferred response above the loser.

5.End-to-end trace — one prompt through RLHF

Follow a single user prompt through the full pipeline. The important thing to track is not only the text, but also who supplies the learning signal at each stage.

What the trace reveals

RLHF is not “humans write the final answer.” It is a pipeline for converting sparse human judgments into a reusable reward signal, then using RL search while preventing the policy from wandering too far from normal assistant behavior.

✓ Check yourself — where can bias enter this trace?

At many points: the prompt distribution, who writes demonstrations, which outputs are sampled, which labelers are hired, how guidelines define “helpful” or “harmful,” and whether the reward model generalizes outside the labeled distribution.

6.Scalable oversight: when humans cannot evaluate unaided

The second half of the lecture asks what happens as AI tasks outgrow unaided human evaluation. Leike draws a conceptual graph: AI capability rises with progress, but the level of task humans can reliably evaluate does not automatically rise. Once the model operates above that line, naive RLHF can reward what merely looks good to humans.

Failure mode — Goodharting and deception

When a proxy becomes the optimization target, the system can learn the proxy rather than the thing we meant. In RLHF, the proxy is human preference feedback or a reward model trained from it. A sufficiently capable model might learn to tell evaluators what they want to hear, hide flaws, or exploit blind spots.

6.1 Critiques and targeted perturbations

Leike’s concrete example is AI-written critiques for summaries. The hard part is measuring whether assistance works, because real hard tasks lack obvious ground truth. The trick he describes is targeted perturbation: start with a response, have a human insert a subtle but real flaw, then test whether assisted evaluators catch it more often.

Key equation — an oversight evaluation metric

$$ \text{assist gain}=P(\text{catch flaw}\mid\text{human+AI})-P(\text{catch flaw}\mid\text{human alone}) $$

In the critique work Leike cites, AI assistance helped humans find about 50% more planted flaws in summaries. The point is not that current critiques are perfect; it is that we can empirically test whether assistance improves evaluation.

✓ Check yourself — why are many bad critiques still useful?

Because the human remains the judge. A critique assistant can be noisy, but if even some critiques point to flaws the human would miss, the evaluator can discard the irrelevant ones and inspect the useful ones.

7.Extensions, alternatives, and open failure modes

The lecture centers on RLHF and scalable oversight, but the same alignment problem has spawned related methods. Instruction tuning scales the SFT idea across many tasks. Constitutional AI / RLAIF replaces some direct human comparisons with AI feedback guided by written principles. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), developed after this talk, optimizes a policy directly from preferences without separately training and then optimizing a reward model.

Key equation — DPO in one line

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}=-\log\sigma\left(\beta\left[\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_w\mid x)}-\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_l\mid x)}\right]\right) $$

DPO keeps the same preference-pair spirit but folds the reward/KL tradeoff into a supervised-looking loss against a reference policy \(\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}\).

Outer versus inner alignment

Outer alignment asks whether the training signal captures what we actually want. Inner alignment asks whether the learned model internally pursues that intended objective, especially under distribution shift. Leike’s stance in the Q&A is pragmatic: build a reliable outer signal first, then use it to train and test behavior on new distributions.

✓ Check yourself — why is “use AI to judge AI” not a complete answer?

Because the assistant can bias the human, miss the key flaw, or itself be misaligned. The scalable-oversight plan needs experiments with ground truth, red-teaming, interpretability where useful, diverse preference input, and deployment monitoring.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
AlignmentTraining and deploying AI systems so their behavior follows human intent and preferences.
RLHFReinforcement Learning from Human Feedback: SFT, reward modeling from preferences, then RL optimization.
SFTSupervised fine-tuning on human-written demonstrations of desired behavior.
Reward modelA learned scorer that predicts which outputs humans would prefer.
PPOProximal Policy Optimization, an RL algorithm used to update the policy while limiting overly large changes.
KL penaltyA cost for moving the policy too far from a reference model, usually the SFT model.
