Aligning Open Language Models

A timeline-plus-recipe for how open builders reproduced, simplified, and debated the ChatGPT-style alignment stack.

🎓 Nathan Lambert · Allen Institute for AI ⏱ ≈1 hr ▶ Watch
Why this matters

After ChatGPT, the central question was not only “can closed labs align assistants?” It was: can an open ecosystem reproduce enough of the recipe to make useful, inspectable, reusable chat models? Nathan Lambert’s answer is a history of artifacts: Alpaca, Vicuna, Dolly, OpenAssistant, Llama-2-Chat, Zephyr, Tülu, Starling, and the evaluation culture that made progress visible.

This note complements Note 12 on language and human alignment and Note 22 on the helpful-chatbot recipe. Those explain the closed-lab recipe; this one explains how the open recipe became a community craft.

Running analogy — opening a restaurant recipe

Think of a base language model as a powerful kitchen: it can cook many things, but it does not know the house style. Alignment is the recipe book, tasting panel, and quality-control loop. Closed labs ran private kitchens. Open alignment is the community reconstructing the recipe from public ingredients, shared tasting notes, and repeated cook-offs.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before the timeline. Each term appears repeatedly in the lecture.

  • Language model — a next-token predictor trained by autoregressive loss; alignment changes how that predictor behaves in conversations.
  • SFT / instruction tuning — continue training on prompt–answer examples so the model follows instructions and chat templates; see Note 22.
  • Preference data — triples like \(x, y_w, y_l\): prompt, preferred answer, rejected answer; they turn taste into trainable signal.
  • Reward model / RLHF — learn a scalar reward from comparisons, then optimize the model with an RL method such as PPO while staying near a reference model; see Note 12.
  • Logistic \(\sigma\) — \(\sigma(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})\) converts a score difference into a probability, used in preference losses.
  • KL / reference model — a “do not drift too far from the old chef” anchor that preserves fluency, format, and broad capabilities while optimizing preferences.

1.Why open alignment became a field

Lambert starts before ChatGPT because the punchline depends on the long arc: Shannon-style sequence modeling, transformers, GPT/BERT scaling, GPT-3 prompting, and then ChatGPT. The technical surprise was not that next-token prediction mattered; it was that post-training could turn a raw predictor into something people experienced as an assistant.

1.1 The product recipe becomes a weights recipe

The InstructGPT / ChatGPT-style stack had three ingredients: a pretrained base model, supervised demonstrations, and preference optimization. Closed labs could add private data, product moderation, and hidden evaluations. Open builders had to ask a harder question: which parts can be reproduced with released weights, public data, public code, and modest compute?

Key equation — the raw ingredient is next-token likelihood

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}}(\theta)=-\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log \pi_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t}) $$

Here \(\pi_\theta\) is the model, \(x_t\) is the next token, and \(x_{<t}\) is the prefix. Pretraining makes the kitchen broadly capable; it does not by itself specify “be helpful, conversational, and safe.”

Common misconception

“Open ChatGPT” means open-sourcing a product. Lambert stresses that ChatGPT is a model plus product system: prompts, filters, interfaces, policies, monitoring, and private data. The open ecosystem usually releases weights, datasets, code, and reports—not the full closed-lab product stack.

✓ Check yourself — what was the open community trying to reproduce?

Not merely the UI of ChatGPT. It was trying to reproduce the post-training behavior: instruction following, chat formatting, helpfulness, lower refusal weirdness, and preference-shaped responses using open weights and shareable data.

2.The first open wave: instruction tuning as recipe copying

The first open breakthroughs did not require full RLHF. They asked a simpler question: if a stronger model can answer many instructions, can we distill those demonstrations into a smaller open base model?

2.1 Distillation, human data, and access

Alpaca used 52k self-instruct-style examples generated from text-davinci-003. Vicuna raised the bar with ShareGPT conversations—messier and legally gray, but closer to what people actually asked ChatGPT. Koala mixed data sources. Dolly and OpenAssistant mattered because they added human-written, openly released data. QLoRA / Guanaco lowered the memory barrier so more people could fine-tune 7B–65B models.

Key equation — SFT copies demonstrated answers

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}}(\theta)=-\mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\mathcal{D}_{\text{inst}}}\left[\sum_{t}\log \pi_\theta(y_t\mid x,y_{<t})\right] $$

\(x\) is an instruction, \(y\) is a demonstrated answer, and \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{inst}}\) is the instruction dataset. The model learns the chat format and answer style by imitating examples.

Why this worked

In the restaurant analogy, Alpaca and Vicuna did not discover new cooking physics. They obtained many plated examples from a strong chef, then trained apprentices to imitate the plating and response style. That was enough to make open models feel dramatically more assistant-like.

✓ Check yourself — why was ShareGPT powerful and problematic?

Powerful: it contained real ChatGPT-style conversations, giving open builders a richer prompt distribution than synthetic seed tasks. Problematic: many conversations were scraped or redistributed in a legally and consent-wise gray area, so the data-transparency story was weak.

3.Preference optimization: RLHF versus DPO

Instruction tuning says “copy this answer.” Preference optimization says “between these two possible answers, move toward the one people prefer.” This is the point where alignment becomes tasting: the community needs comparisons, judges, and optimization methods.

3.1 RLHF: reward model + PPO

The classic RLHF recipe learns a reward model \(r_\phi(x,y)\) from pairwise preferences. Then PPO updates the policy \(\pi_\theta\) to get higher reward while staying near a reference policy \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\). Llama-2-Chat was important because Meta openly described a serious RLHF pipeline; StableVicuna and XwinLM were earlier open signs that PPO could work.

Key equation — reward-model preference loss

$$ \mathcal{L}_{RM}=-\log\sigma\big(r(x,y_w)-r(x,y_l)\big) $$

\(y_w\) is the preferred response, \(y_l\) is the rejected response, \(r(x,y)\) is the reward score, and \(\sigma\) maps the reward gap to the model’s probability that \(y_w\) should win.

Key equation — RLHF objective with a KL anchor

$$ \max_{\pi_\theta}\;\mathbb{E}_{x,y\sim\pi_\theta}\left[r_\phi(x,y)\right] -\beta\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\,\|\,\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot\mid x)\right) $$

The reward term says “please the tasting panel.” The KL term says “do not become a bizarre chef who wins the panel by exploiting the scoring rubric.” \(\beta\) controls how strong that anchor is.

3.2 DPO: the open simplifier

Direct Preference Optimization reframes the same preference signal so the policy can be trained directly from \((x,y_w,y_l)\) pairs. No separate reward model has to be trained and no PPO loop has to be stabilized. Lambert emphasizes why this mattered socially: DPO was simple, scaled through normal language-model training libraries, and made progress feel reachable.

Key equation — DPO uses the policy as an implicit reward

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}=-\log\sigma\left(\beta\left[\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_w\mid x)}-\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_l\mid x)}\right]\right) $$

The bracket is a relative log-probability advantage: raise the preferred response \(y_w\), lower the rejected response \(y_l\), but measure both relative to the reference model. In this sense, DPO reparameterizes the reward implicitly via policy log probabilities.

The DPO era became visible through Zephyr, which used Mistral plus UltraFeedback and very low learning rates, and Tülu, which showed a DPO-style recipe could scale to 70B. The lecture’s nuance is important: DPO simplified the recipe, but PPO and other RL methods did not disappear. Starling, SteerLM, and later Llama 3-style multi-stage recipes kept reward models, rejection sampling, DPO, and PPO in the same conversation.

✓ Check yourself — why did DPO spread so quickly in open labs?

Because it converts preference tuning into a normal supervised-looking loss over log probabilities. Open teams could reuse SFT infrastructure instead of building a stable reward-model-plus-PPO pipeline.

4.Evaluation and open culture

Once new models appeared weekly, “I tried it and it feels good” was not enough. Open alignment needed shared tasting tables: fast evaluations for builders and slower public leaderboards for the ecosystem.

4.1 Leaderboards: signal, not truth

Lambert names the evaluation wave: Chatbot Arena for human pairwise votes and Elo-like rankings, AlpacaEval for LLM-as-a-judge pairwise comparisons, MT-Bench for GPT-4-style scoring of multi-turn prompts, and the Open LLM Leaderboard for broad discovery. They were valuable because they gave signal in minutes or days; they were limited because they could saturate, hide biases, reward length, and invite contamination or gaming.

Common misconception

“A higher leaderboard score means the model is aligned.” A score can be useful and still incomplete. It may reward verbosity, overlap with training data, judge-model quirks, or benchmark-specific style. Alignment also includes licensing, data provenance, safety boundaries, multilingual coverage, and whether users can reproduce the result.

4.2 Reproducibility, licensing, and safety are part of the recipe

Open alignment is cultural as much as algorithmic. The strongest open releases document data mixtures, training stages, compute, licenses, and evaluation scripts. The hardest open problems remain data quality and consent, compute access, evaluation gaming, the lag behind frontier closed systems, and safety: open weights are not inherently unsafe, but capability discovery and misuse risk make release strategy a real research and policy question.

What Lambert thinks still bottlenecks open alignment

Data. The lecture repeatedly returns to the same point: open builders have a small set of widely used preference datasets—Anthropic HH, OpenAssistant, UltraFeedback, Nectar—and need more diverse, human, licensed data. Synthetic and distilled data help, but can be repetitive and brittle.

✓ Check yourself — name one evaluation benefit and one evaluation risk.

Benefit: quick feedback lets builders compare recipes without running expensive human studies. Risk: models can overfit to judge preferences, contaminated prompts, or benchmark style, giving a score increase that does not transfer to real users.

5.Worked example — DPO vs reward-model preference

Take one prompt \(x\), one preferred answer \(y_w\), and one rejected answer \(y_l\). Suppose a reward model assigns implied rewards \(r_w=1.5\) and \(r_l=0.4\). How confident is the preference model that \(y_w\) should beat \(y_l\)?

Step-by-step with real numbers

First compute the reward gap:

$$ \Delta r = r_w-r_l = 1.5-0.4=1.1. $$

Convert the gap into a preference probability with the logistic function:

$$ \sigma(1.1)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-1.1}}\approx\frac{1}{1+0.333}\approx0.75. $$

Now compute the reward-model loss:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{RM}=-\log(0.75)\approx0.288\approx0.29. $$

Interpretation: the reward model already gives the preferred answer a decent advantage, so the loss is moderate. DPO uses the same comparison shape, but instead of first learning explicit rewards \(r_w,r_l\), it pushes \(\log\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)\) up relative to \(\log\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)\), while comparing both to a reference model.

✓ Check yourself — if the reward gap were 0, what probability and loss would you get?

\(\sigma(0)=0.5\), so the model is indifferent. The loss is \(-\log 0.5\approx0.693\), larger than 0.29 because the preference is not yet well explained.

✓ Check yourself — what does the reference model prevent in DPO?

It prevents unconstrained drift. Without the reference ratio, the policy could raise the preferred response in strange ways, collapse style, or exploit the comparison data. The reference model acts like a KL-style anchor to the SFT model’s fluency and broad behavior.

6.End-to-end trace — one open model from base to chat

Follow one hypothetical open 7B base model through the modern recipe. The exact datasets vary by project, but this is the skeleton Lambert keeps returning to.

StageInputOutput/Data
0 · base LMPretrained open weights; web-scale next-token trainingbase LM
1 · SFT on instruction dataHuman, synthetic, or distilled prompt–answer examples; chat templateinstruction-following SFT model
2 · collect / curate preference pairsPrompts plus multiple candidate answers, ranked by humans, GPT-4-style judges, reward models, or community data(x, y_w, y_l) preference dataset
3 · DPO or RM+PPODPO trains directly on pair log-probs; RLHF trains a reward model, then updates with PPO; rejection sampling can keep best-of-n answerspreference-tuned chat policy
4 · eval on Arena / MT-BenchHuman battles, LLM-as-judge scores, refusal tests, safety probes, contamination checksscorecards plus failure cases
5 · released chat modelWeights, model card, data notes, license, evaluation report, demo endpointreleased chat model
Why DPO simplified the open recipe

DPO removed two fragile engineering loops: training a separate reward model and stabilizing RL updates. In the restaurant analogy, it let the kitchen train directly from “dish A beat dish B” tasting cards, instead of first building a separate critic and then running a reinforcement-learning service around that critic.

✓ Check yourself — where can synthetic data enter this trace?

At SFT, by generating prompt–answer examples from a stronger model; at preference collection, by generating candidate answers or judge labels; and at rejection sampling, by sampling many completions and keeping the best scored ones.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
AlignmentPost-training a model so its responses better match user and developer preferences: helpfulness, style, safety, honesty, and task behavior.
Instruction tuning / SFTTraining on prompt–answer demonstrations so a base model learns to respond in an assistant format.
Self-InstructA bootstrapping method: start with a few tasks, ask a stronger model for more tasks and answers, then fine-tune on the expanded set.
DistillationUsing outputs from a stronger teacher model as training data for a smaller or more open student model.
Preference pairA prompt with two candidate answers, one marked preferred and one rejected: \(x,y_w,y_l\).
Reward modelA model that maps a prompt and answer to a scalar score used as feedback for RLHF.
PPOProximal Policy Optimization, an RL algorithm often used to optimize a language model against a learned reward while limiting update size.
DPODirect Preference Optimization, a loss that trains the policy directly from preference pairs using a reference model.
Rejection sampling / best-of-nGenerate several answers, score or rank them, and keep the best; useful for data generation and sometimes inference.
Chatbot Arena / EloA public human-vote arena where models are compared pairwise and assigned Elo-like rankings.
ContaminationWhen evaluation prompts or answers leak into training data, making scores look better than true generalization.
