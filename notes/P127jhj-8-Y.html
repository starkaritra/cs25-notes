Transformers United: The Deep-Learning Models that Revolutionized NLP, CV & RL

An overview lecture: where transformers came from, why they took over every field of AI, and what's still unsolved.

🎓 Instructors: Div Garg, Steven Feng, Rylan Schaeffer (+ Andrej Karpathy guest) ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch on YouTube
Why this lecture matters

In ~2011, an AI researcher in vision, language, and speech each spoke a different technical language and used different models. A decade later they all use one architecture — the Transformer — copy-pasted across images, text, protein folding, and game-playing. This lecture is the map of how that convergence happened. Everything else in CS25 is a branch off this trunk.

Running analogy for the whole course

Think of a transformer as a room full of experts passing sticky-notes. Every token (word, image patch, game state) is a person in the room. On each round, everyone looks at everyone else, decides whom to listen to, and updates their own notes. "Attention" is just the rule for how much each person listens to each other person. Hold onto this image — every equation below is a formalization of it.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these; dive into any that feel shaky.

  • Dot product — sums the products of two vectors' entries; a scalar measure of how aligned they are. Attention uses it to score relevance.
  • Softmax — turns a vector of scores into positive weights that sum to 1 (a probability distribution).
  • RNN hidden state \(h_t\) — a recurrent net's running memory vector after reading \(t\) tokens.
  • Token — one unit the model processes (a word-piece here).

1.The attention timeline

The lecture opens with a history, because the design of the Transformer only makes sense as a reaction to what came before. Here is the arc, from "prehistoric" recurrent nets to the generative era.

1.1 Before transformers: RNNs and seq2seq

To process a sequence (a sentence, a time series), the pre-2017 tool was the Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) and its gated variants, the LSTM and GRU. An RNN reads one token at a time, carrying a hidden state \(h_t\) — a compressed memory of everything seen so far:

Key equation — the recurrence

$$h_t = f(W_h\,h_{t-1} + W_x\,x_t)$$

The state at step \(t\) depends on the previous state \(h_{t-1}\). That single arrow — "step \(t\) waits for step \(t\!-\!1\)" — is the source of every problem below.

What worked: RNNs encode recent history well.
What broke: two things.

  • Long-range context loss. Take the lecture's example: "I grew up in France … I speak fluent ___." To fill the blank with French, the model must connect a word 15+ steps back. In an RNN that signal has to survive being repeatedly overwritten in \(h_t\) — it usually doesn't.
  • No parallelism. Because \(h_t\) needs \(h_{t-1}\), you must process the sequence strictly left-to-right. You cannot use a GPU's thousands of cores to read all words at once.

1.2 The encoder bottleneck

For translation, the 2014 sequence-to-sequence design used two RNNs: an encoder reads the English sentence into a single fixed vector, and a decoder generates French from that vector.

The core failure

Compressing a 40-word sentence into one vector loses information — like summarizing a book in a single sentence and then trying to translate the book from only that sentence. Performance collapsed on long inputs. The fix to this bottleneck is the entire origin of attention.

2.The birth of attention

The idea (Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio, 2015): instead of forcing the decoder to rely on one summary vector, let it look back at every encoder state and choose what to focus on as it generates each word.

2.1 Bahdanau's "soft search"

When the decoder is about to emit a word, it forms its context vector \(c_i\) as a weighted average of all encoder hidden states \(h_j\). The weights \(\alpha_{ij}\) say how relevant source word \(j\) is to target word \(i\):

Key equation — soft attention (the ancestor of everything)

$$c_i = \sum_{j} \alpha_{ij}\,h_j, \qquad \alpha_{ij} = \frac{\exp(e_{ij})}{\sum_k \exp(e_{ik})}, \qquad e_{ij} = \text{score}(s_{i-1}, h_j)$$

Read it as: score every source word against my current decoding state \(\to\) softmax into a probability distribution (the "how much I listen" weights) \(\to\) take the weighted blend. This is the sticky-note rule from the analogy, written down.

Intuition

Attention turns "remember everything in one vector" into "keep everything, and dynamically retrieve the relevant bits on demand." The softmax makes it a soft (differentiable) lookup — so it can be learned by gradient descent, unlike a hard "pick word #7" choice.

2.2 The origin story (from the lecture)

Karpathy shares an email from Dzmitry Bahdanau on where the mechanism came from. He was looking to kill the encoder bottleneck, and drew on learning English in middle school: your gaze shifts back and forth between the source and target sentence as you translate. He encoded that "where do I look now?" as a softmax-weighted average — and it worked on the first try. The name "attention" was suggested by Yoshua Bengio on a final pass; the working title had been "RNN Search."

Historical note worth remembering

"Attention Is All You Need" was almost "RNN Search Is All You Need." The mechanism predates the famous 2017 paper by two years — 2017's contribution was to throw the RNN away and keep only attention.

3.2017: "Attention Is All You Need"

In Bahdanau's work, attention was a small helper bolted onto a bidirectional RNN. The 2017 Transformer paper made a radical cut: delete the recurrence entirely. If attention lets any position talk to any other position directly, you don't need to pass information step-by-step through a hidden state at all.

This bought two things RNNs could never have:

  • Direct long-range links. "France … French" is now one attention edge, not 15 hops through a decaying state.
  • Full parallelism. All tokens are processed simultaneously — perfect for GPUs, which is why transformers could be scaled to billions of parameters.
The price (foreshadowing §6)

"Every token attends to every token" means \(n\) tokens produce \(n^2\) attention scores. This quadratic cost in sequence length is the single biggest limitation the rest of the field keeps trying to fix (long context, efficient attention).

✓ Check yourself — why can't an RNN be parallelized across time but a transformer can?

An RNN's state \(h_t=f(h_{t-1},x_t)\) needs \(h_{t-1}\) before it can compute \(h_t\), forcing strict left-to-right order. Self-attention computes all token interactions from the full input at once — no token's computation waits on another's — so all positions run in parallel on a GPU.

4.The convergence thesis

Karpathy's central historical point: AI didn't just adopt a useful tool — every subfield's architecture collapsed onto the same one.

The provocative hint

Karpathy notes the cortex looks remarkably uniform — visual, auditory, and other regions share the same basic circuitry, differing mostly in "hyperparameters." That one homogeneous architecture handles vision, sound, and language in the brain is at least suggestive that a single general learning algorithm (maybe transformer-like) could do the same. He flags this as speculation, not fact — good epistemic hygiene to copy.

The unifying trick

To apply a transformer to a new domain you only change how you chop the input into tokens and how you feed them in. The core "room of experts passing sticky-notes" is identical. That is the whole reason one architecture spread everywhere.

6.What's still unsolved

The lecture closes with the frontier — the same problems that motivate most current research:

  • Quadratic attention cost. \(O(n^2)\) in sequence length makes very long inputs expensive. → efficient/linear attention, state-space models (later CS25).
  • Limited context / no long-term memory. Early models capped near ~4k tokens; conversations are "short-lived." → retrieval-augmented models, longer contexts.
  • Controllability. Outputs are stochastic — re-run ChatGPT, get a different answer. → steering, decoding controls, alignment.
  • Alignment with human values (and brains). → RLHF (how ChatGPT is trained), interpretability.
  • Domain specialists & mixtures of experts. Instead of one model that knows everything, specialized "DoctorGPT / LawyerGPT" experts routed via a mixture-of-experts. → MoE lecture.

7.Worked numeric micro-example

Here is the soft-attention mechanism from §2 with only two source states, so the arithmetic is visible.

Tiny soft-attention calculation

Suppose the decoder scores two encoder hidden states with raw scores:

$$e = [2.0,\;0.5]$$

Softmax exponentiates and normalizes:

$$\exp(e) = [e^{2.0},\;e^{0.5}] \approx [7.39,\;1.65]$$

$$\sum_k \exp(e_k) \approx 7.39 + 1.65 = 9.04$$

$$\alpha = \left[\frac{7.39}{9.04},\;\frac{1.65}{9.04}\right] \approx [0.82,\;0.18]$$

With tiny 2-D encoder states \(h_1=[1,0]\) and \(h_2=[0,1]\), the context is:

$$c = 0.82\,h_1 + 0.18\,h_2 = 0.82[1,0] + 0.18[0,1] \approx [0.82,\;0.18]$$

✓ Check yourself — what happens to \(\alpha\) if the second score rises to 2.0?

The scores become \(e=[2.0,2.0]\). Equal scores produce equal softmax weights, so \(\alpha=[0.5,0.5]\): the decoder listens to both source states equally.

8.End-to-end trace: France → French

Now trace the lecture's long-range example through the attention lens.

StepWhat happensState
Input tokensThe sentence is split into word-piece tokens.I, grew, up, in, France, …, I, speak, fluent, ___
Encoder states \(h_j\)Each input position gets a hidden state that stores what that token means in context.\(h_{\text{France}}\) carries the important country clue.
Decoder scores each \(h_j\)At the blank, the decoder asks which earlier states help predict the next word.\(e_{\text{France}}\) becomes much larger than scores for filler words.
Softmax weightsThe scores become attention weights.Most \(\alpha_j\) lands on France.
Context vectorThe decoder forms a weighted blend of encoder states.\(c \approx\) mostly \(h_{\text{France}}\), with small contributions from other tokens.
PredictionThe output layer uses that context to choose the blank.French
Intuition

One attention edge beats 15 RNN hops because the "France" signal does not have to survive repeated overwrites in a running state. The decoder can point straight back to the relevant token, pull its state into the context vector, and predict "French" from that direct evidence.
