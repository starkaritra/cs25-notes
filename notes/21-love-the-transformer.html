How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Transformer

Ashish Vaswani tells the transformer story as a story of consolidation: from hand-built AI and NLP pipelines to one parallel, content-routing architecture that scales into modern LLMs, agents, tools, and data-center-sized computers.

🎓 Ashish Vaswani ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

The original AI dream was a single machine that could simulate intelligence. For decades, practical AI moved the other way: specialized pipelines, rule systems, and task-specific components. Vaswani's core claim is that the transformer helped reverse that fragmentation. Its central trick is not magic prose generation; it is parallel content-based routing — every token can look at other tokens and decide what information to pull in.

Running analogy

Think of a transformer as a busy research library. Each token is a researcher with a question. Keys are catalog cards, values are the books on the shelves, and attention is the act of walking to the most relevant shelves, copying useful pages, and returning with a better note. Multi-head attention means each researcher has several assistants, each searching for a different kind of clue.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before entering the library.

  • Dot product — a score for vector alignment; here it asks, “does this query match that key?”
  • Softmax — turns arbitrary scores into positive weights that sum to 1, so a token can blend several values.
  • Sequence-to-sequence — maps an input sequence to an output sequence, the old workhorse for translation, summarization, and dialogue.
  • Residual connection — adds a layer's update back to its input, preserving a highway for information and gradients.
  • GPU/TPU matrix multiplication — modern accelerators love dense matrix products; architecture choices that fit them train and serve better.

1.The old dream, the fragmented field

Vaswani starts not with the 2017 paper, but with the 1955 Dartmouth proposal. The founders hoped every aspect of intelligence could be described precisely enough for a machine to simulate it, and they thought a summer of work by selected scientists might make major progress. The joke lands because the goal was grand, but two assumptions were wrong: intelligence resisted clean hand-written rules, and machine capacity mattered far more than expected.

1.1 Dartmouth's wager

The lecture frames transformers as one answer to that long-running wager. Instead of writing down all the rules for intelligence, we build systems that learn useful rules from data. Solomonoff-style compression and self-supervised learning appear in the background: if a model predicts the world well, it must internalize some structure of the world.

The thesis in one sentence

The transformer is a general substrate for moving information around: once the routing mechanism is flexible, parallel, and accelerator-friendly, many tasks can be expressed as “learn representations, then route the right pieces to the right places.”

1.2 NLP before consolidation

Before neural end-to-end systems, a machine-translation system was a cathedral of components: word alignment, phrase extraction, scoring, re-ranking, and a small neural language model hidden somewhere in the pipeline. Even research conferences were “verticalized” into morphology, dialogue, discourse, sentiment, and translation tracks. Word embeddings began the consolidation by giving words distributed representations; contextual models went further by letting king mean different things in “king of Persia” and “the emperor has no clothes.”

Common misconception

It is tempting to say “transformers replaced linguistics with scale.” The lecture's subtler point is that they replaced many brittle, manually connected modules with a trainable substrate. Linguistic structure did not disappear; it became something the model could learn, route, and reuse.

✓ Check yourself — what was being consolidated?

Not merely code. The field was consolidating task formulations, representations, and data-driven learning: many NLP problems became variable-length sequence problems, then sequence-to-sequence problems, then attention-based representation-learning problems.

2.Why recurrence and convolutions were not enough

The pre-transformer era already had powerful neural models. LSTMs could learn counters and grammar-like state; Google Neural Machine Translation showed that sequence-to-sequence systems could be practical. The frustration was architectural: the model still read left-to-right through a narrow state bottleneck.

2.1 LSTMs: powerful but sequential

An LSTM “eats” a word, updates a hidden state, then moves to the next word. That hidden state must carry local details and long-distance dependencies. To represent the tenth word, the encoder must process words one through nine first. Translation also decodes autoregressively. Reading and writing both become serial processes.

Key equation — recurrence as a bottleneck

$$h_t = f_ heta(x_t, h_{t-1})$$

More plainly: the representation at position \(t\), called \(h_t\), is computed from the current token \(x_t\) and the previous state \(h_{t-1}\). The function \(f_\theta\) is learned. Because \(h_t\) depends on \(h_{t-1}\), positions cannot all be processed at once.

2.2 Convolutions: parallel but local

Convolutions looked promising because every position can look at local neighbors in parallel, and GPUs already compute them well. ByteNet, ConvS2S, and WaveNet showed the approach could work. But a small convolutional receptive field grows with depth: a word only “sees” faraway words after many layers. Attention offered a direct path from any token to any other token in one layer.

Library analogy checkpoint

An LSTM is one librarian walking shelf by shelf with a notebook. A convolution is many librarians checking nearby shelves. Self-attention is every librarian searching the whole catalog by content, in parallel.

✓ Check yourself — why did convolutions not fully solve long-distance language?

They parallelize local computation, but distant tokens need enough layers for the receptive field to reach them. Self-attention can score every token pair in one layer, so the path length between faraway words is shorter.

3.The transformer block

The central move is simple: let a sentence attend to itself. Each token creates a query, a key, and a value. The query asks what it needs; keys advertise what other positions contain; values carry the content to be pooled. This made attention a content-based pooling operation that could be written as matrix multiplication.

3.1 Attention as content-based pooling

Key equation — scaled dot-product attention

$$\operatorname{Attention}(Q,K,V)=\operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} + M\right)V$$

\(Q\) contains one query per position, \(K\) contains keys, \(V\) contains values, \(d_k\) is the key/query dimension, and \(M\) is an optional mask, such as a causal mask in a decoder. The matrix \(QK^\top\) scores every query against every key; dividing by \(\sqrt{d_k}\) keeps logits from growing too large; softmax converts scores into mixing weights.

3.2 Multi-head attention and residual blocks

Single-head attention can blur roles together. In the lecture's “the cat licked the owner's hands” example, a single head that needs both who and what might average two useful positions. Multi-head attention restores selectivity: one subspace can attend to the subject, another to the object, another to local syntax, all for roughly the same overall model width.

Key equation — multi-head attention

$$\operatorname{MHA}(X)=\operatorname{Concat}(H_1,\ldots,H_h)W^O,\qquad H_i=\operatorname{Attention}(XW_i^Q,XW_i^K,XW_i^V)$$

\(X\) is the matrix of token representations, \(h\) is the number of heads, \(W_i^Q,W_i^K,W_i^V\) are learned projections for head \(i\), and \(W^O\) mixes the heads back together. Each head searches the library through a different lens.

Common misconception

Attention is not “the model thinking in English.” It is a differentiable routing operation. Some heads become interpretable, but Vaswani warns that reading attention maps can become tea-leaf reading unless backed by stronger mechanistic evidence.

✓ Check yourself — why divide by \(\sqrt{d_k}\)?

If query and key dimensions grow, raw dot products tend to grow in magnitude. Very large logits make softmax nearly one-hot and gradients unstable. Scaling by \(\sqrt{d_k}\) keeps the score variance in a healthier range.

4.What changed after the original paper

The lecture's middle act is a guided tour of changes that “stuck.” The original transformer solved enough of the problem to win on WMT translation and parsing, but later systems changed the block to train deeper, represent position better, serve faster, and handle longer contexts.

4.1 Position: from sinusoids to RoPE

Self-attention by itself is permutation-invariant: if you shuffle tokens and do not tell the model where they came from, attention has no built-in ordering. The original paper used sinusoidal absolute positions and hoped relative distances would be easy to learn. Later work made the relative-distance signal more explicit: learned relative attention, ALiBi biases, and rotary position embeddings.

Key equation — relative bias idea

$$\ell_{ij}=\frac{q_i \cdot k_j}{\sqrt{d_k}} + b(i-j)$$

\(\ell_{ij}\) is the attention logit from position \(i\) to position \(j\). The term \(b(i-j)\) depends on relative distance, so the model can distinguish “nearby,” “far behind,” and “far ahead” even when token content is similar. RoPE reaches a related effect by rotating query/key dimensions according to absolute position so their dot product depends on relative offset.

The Music Transformer example makes this concrete. Music needs motifs at several scales: local rhythm, phrase repetition, and long-range return. Vaswani plays examples where vanilla positional encodings drift, while relative attention better preserves structure.

✓ Check yourself — why is position not optional?

Without position, “dog bites man” and “man bites dog” contain the same tokens. Attention can compare content, but it needs position information to model order, roles, rhythm, and causality.

4.2 Long context and the physics of memory

Full attention is expressive but expensive in sequence length. More importantly, not all FLOPs are equal. Attention can spend too much time moving logits, probabilities, keys, and values through GPU memory rather than doing compute. Vaswani repeatedly returns to the practical rule: make physics your friend.

Key equation — length cost

$$\text{attention logits per head} = n\times n, \qquad \text{compute} \approx O(n^2 d_k)$$

\(n\) is the sequence length and \(d_k\) is the head dimension. Long context hurts because the logit matrix grows quadratically, and because writing/reading those intermediate tensors can dominate runtime.

Why FlashAttention felt inevitable in hindsight

Softmax can be computed online. If the GPU streams blocks of \(Q,K,V\), updates partial softmax statistics, and avoids writing the full \(n\times n\) logit matrix to high-bandwidth memory, the same math becomes much faster.

✓ Check yourself — why can grouped-query attention be a good compromise?

Multi-query attention uses one shared key/value head, saving KV-cache memory but reducing expressivity. Grouped-query attention shares keys/values within groups of query heads, retaining more specialization while still shrinking inference memory traffic.

5.The bigger thesis: a new computer, not the final computer

The closing sections return to Dartmouth. Modern LLMs are closer to the single general machine than the 2009 MT pipeline ever was: one model can follow prompts, blend knowledge, call tools, and help program agents. But Vaswani does not declare the architecture finished. He emphasizes scaling laws, data quality, quantization, retrieval, tool use, adaptive reasoning, and the fact that a frontier transformer is now less like a Colab notebook and more like a data center.

Takeaway stance

Love the transformer, but do not worship it. The durable ideas are likely content-based routing, residual representation streams, accelerator-aware dense computation, and scalable self-supervised learning. Future architectures may improve the scaling laws, but they will probably keep some of these invariants.

In Q&A, the lecture also touches non-autoregressive decoding. The dream was to read and write in parallel, but parallel generation must learn which words are conditionally independent and in what order to lock down modes. Autoregressive left-to-right decoding supplies that ordering for free; speculative decoding became the practical compromise.

✓ Check yourself — why do tools not contradict the consolidation story?

A model should not waste billions of FLOPs doing arithmetic or database lookup that external software can do exactly. Tools are interfaces to capabilities; some may later be absorbed into models, while others remain better as external modules.

6.Worked example — one tiny attention lookup by hand

Numbers make the library analogy precise. Suppose the token it needs to decide whether to copy information from three previous tokens: cat, mat, and slept. Use one 2-D query and three 2-D keys/values.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Let $$q=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\end{bmatrix},\quad K=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix},\quad V=\begin{bmatrix}10&0\\0&10\\6&6\end{bmatrix},\quad d_k=2.$$ Raw dot products are $$qK^\top=[1,1,2].$$ Scale by \(\sqrt{2}\): $$s=\left[\frac{1}{1.414},\frac{1}{1.414},\frac{2}{1.414}\right]\approx[0.707,0.707,1.414].$$ Softmax uses exponentials: $$e^s\approx[2.028,2.028,4.113],\qquad \sum e^s=8.169.$$ So the attention weights are $$a\approx[0.248,0.248,0.504].$$ Finally blend values: $$aV=0.248[10,0]+0.248[0,10]+0.504[6,6]\approx[5.50,5.50].$$

The query matched the third key most strongly, so the output mostly copies the third value, but still keeps a quarter of each other source. That is attention as soft retrieval, not hard selection.

✓ Check yourself — if the third value became \([8,2]\), what would the output be?

Keep the same weights \([0.248,0.248,0.504]\). The output is \(0.248[10,0]+0.248[0,10]+0.504[8,2]=[2.48+4.032,2.48+1.008]\approx[6.51,3.49]\).

7.End-to-end trace — follow one translation input

Now follow one concrete sentence through the architecture Vaswani motivates: not every numeric value, but the state and shape at each stage.

What the trace reveals

The transformer separates two jobs: routing decides which positions should exchange information, while feed-forward layers transform the routed content. Residual paths let every layer add an update without erasing the evolving representation.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Content-based routingMoving information according to similarity between learned representations, not fixed position alone.
QueryThe vector representation of “what this position is looking for.”
KeyThe vector representation of “what this position offers for matching.”
ValueThe information vector that gets copied/blended after attention weights are computed.
Residual streamThe running representation that layers update additively, preserving old information while adding new computations.
Pre-LayerNormA transformer block layout where normalization happens before sublayers, making very deep models easier to train.
RoPERotary position embeddings: rotate query/key dimensions by position so attention scores reflect relative distance.
KV cacheStored decoder keys and values reused during autoregressive inference so earlier tokens need not be recomputed.
FlashAttentionAn exact attention algorithm organized to reduce GPU memory traffic by tiling and online softmax.
Speculative decodingGenerate candidate tokens with a small model, then verify them with a larger model to speed inference.
