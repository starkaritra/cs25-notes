Transformers for Audio, Speech & Music

Audio transformers work only after we choose a listenable tokenization: compress a 16,000-samples-per-second stream into spectrogram patches, latent units, or codec codes that attention can afford.

🎓 Prateek Verma, Stanford ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A sentence has maybe 20 word tokens. One second of speech at 16 kHz has 16,000 raw samples; ten seconds has 160,000. If we feed every sample into full self-attention, the attention matrix grows like \(N^2\), so the model spends its memory staring at sample-level dust instead of hearing phonemes, words, beats, or musical phrases.

Running analogy — from microphone tape to sheet music

Raw waveform samples are like a mile-long strip of magnetic tape: faithful, but painfully granular. A spectrogram is the same performance rewritten as sheet music: time runs left to right, frequency runs bottom to top, and meaningful events become visible chunks. Audio transformers mostly succeed by deciding which kind of "sheet music" their attention should read.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before the pipeline; each term will appear quickly.

  • Spectrogram / STFT — slice a waveform into short windows, Fourier-transform each window, then stack magnitudes into a time-frequency image; this is the main bridge from audio to transformer tokens.
  • Sampling rate — the number of amplitude measurements per second, e.g. 16 kHz means 16,000 samples/sec; it determines raw sequence length.
  • ViT patchify — split an image into fixed-size patches, linearly embed each patch, add positions, then use a transformer; AST applies this to spectrogram "images".
  • Self-supervised / contrastive learning — train from unlabeled audio by predicting or distinguishing hidden pieces; Wav2Vec 2.0 and HuBERT use this to learn speech units.
  • Encoder-only vs encoder-decoder — encoder-only models summarize an input for classification/CTC; encoder-decoder models condition a decoder to emit text or tokens, as in Whisper.

1.Why audio strains attention

Prateek's lecture starts with a warning: transformers look universal, but audio arrives at a very different scale than text. The same mechanism that made BERT and GPT powerful becomes expensive when the "sentence" is a continuous pressure wave sampled thousands of times per second.

1.1 Raw samples are too many tokens

Imagine treating a 1-second clip as a 16,000-token sentence. A single attention head would form a 16,000 by 16,000 score table before it even computes values. That is useful if the model truly needs sample-perfect phase, but wasteful for many tasks: an ambulance siren, a spoken phoneme, or a piano chord lasts across many samples.

Key equation — attention cost

$$\text{scores}=\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}},\qquad \text{score entries}=N^2$$

Here \(N\) is the number of tokens and \(d_k\) is the key/query width. For \(N=16{,}000\), one layer-head has \(256{,}000{,}000\) pairwise scores; for ten seconds, \(N=160{,}000\) gives \(25.6\) billion scores.

Common misconception

"Raw audio is the purest input, so it must be best." Raw samples are most faithful, not necessarily most learnable. For recognition, the model often needs robust structure over tens of milliseconds, not a separate token for every tiny amplitude tick.

✓ Check yourself — why is 10 seconds of raw 16 kHz audio worse than 1 second?

The token count grows by 10x, but full attention scores grow by \(10^2=100\)x: \((160{,}000)^2/(16{,}000)^2=100\). That quadratic jump is the reason audio models compress or chunk.

1.2 Audio is continuous and multi-scale

Audio has structure at several clocks at once: samples capture microsecond wiggles; STFT frames capture local frequency content; phonemes and notes last tens to hundreds of milliseconds; words, rhythm, and phrases span seconds. A good transformer input should let attention compare the right scale to the right scale.

The design problem

Tokenization is not preprocessing trivia. It decides what the model can attend to cheaply: waveform ticks, spectrogram patches, learned speech units, or discrete acoustic codes.

2.Representations are the key design choice

Before choosing a model, choose the notes on the page. The lecture moves from Fourier/spectrogram views to learned discrete codes because transformers are strong sequence modelers once audio has been written into a manageable sequence.

2.1 Spectrograms and mel-spectrograms: audio as a time-frequency image

A waveform can be decomposed into sinusoids. But speech and music change over time, so we do not Fourier-transform the entire clip once. We slide a short window across the waveform, transform each window, and stack the results. A mel-spectrogram then remaps frequencies to a perceptual scale closer to human hearing.

Key equation — short-time Fourier transform

$$X(m,k)=\sum_{n=0}^{L-1} x[n+mH]w[n]e^{-j2\pi kn/L}$$

\(x[n]\) is the waveform sample, \(w[n]\) is a window of length \(L\), \(H\) is the hop size between windows, \(m\) indexes time frames, and \(k\) indexes frequency bins. Usually the model sees \(\log |X(m,k)|\) or mel-filtered magnitudes.

The Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST) follows the ViT recipe: split the spectrogram into patches, project each flattened patch to a vector, add positional embeddings, and feed the sequence to a transformer encoder for tagging or classification.

✓ Check yourself — what information is lost when a waveform becomes a magnitude spectrogram?

Magnitude spectrograms usually discard phase. That is often acceptable for classification and recognition features, but it matters for high-fidelity reconstruction, source separation, and generative audio.

2.2 Learned latent and discrete tokens: audio as a language

The other path is to learn a tokenizer. Wav2Vec 2.0 uses a convolutional feature encoder to produce latent speech frames, masks some of them, and trains a transformer to identify the correct quantized target among distractors. VQ-style codebooks and neural audio codecs go further: they map audio to discrete IDs, so generation becomes language modeling over acoustic tokens.

Key equation — contrastive masked prediction

$$\mathcal{L}_m=-\log\frac{\exp(\operatorname{sim}(c_m,q_m)/\kappa)}{\sum_{\tilde{q}\in Q_m}\exp(\operatorname{sim}(c_m,\tilde{q})/\kappa)}$$

At masked time \(m\), \(c_m\) is the transformer's context vector, \(q_m\) is the true quantized target, \(Q_m\) contains the true target plus negative samples, and \(\kappa\) is a temperature. The model learns speech structure without transcripts.

Tradeoff triangle

Resolution keeps fine events; short sequence length makes attention affordable; invertibility lets us reconstruct sound. Spectrogram patches are excellent for recognition, but not perfectly invertible. Codec tokens are compact and generative, but tokenizer quality becomes the ceiling. Raw samples are invertible, but usually too long.

✓ Check yourself — if a codebook has too few audio codes, what happens?

Different sounds collapse to the same ID, so the model becomes robust but blurry: it may miss note changes, phonetic detail, or transient events. Too many codes has the opposite failure: long noisy sequences that are hard to model.

3.Four transformer patterns in audio

Once audio has tokens, the architecture choice follows the task. The lecture emphasizes this buffet: raw generative modeling, language modeling over audio codes, and ViT-like spectrogram classification are all the same attention engine wearing different ears.

3.1 Speech recognition: Whisper and encoder-decoder audio

Whisper turns log-mel spectrogram frames into encoder states and uses a text decoder to emit transcripts, timestamps, or translations. Its practical strength comes from weak supervision at scale: many noisy web audio-transcript pairs, not one tiny curated dataset.

Key equation — conditional transcription

$$p(y\mid x)=\prod_{t=1}^{U}p(y_t\mid y_{<t},\operatorname{Enc}(\operatorname{mel}(x)))$$

\(x\) is waveform audio, \(\operatorname{mel}(x)\) is the log-mel input, \(y_t\) is the next text token, and \(U\) is transcript length. The encoder hears; the decoder writes.

3.2 Audio classification and tagging: AST

For environmental sound, music tags, or event detection, AST uses spectrogram patches like image patches. The head may classify a whole clip ("dog bark") or assign multiple tags ("speech", "music", "siren"). This is the self-driving-car and smartwatch side of audio: recognize what is happening from sound alone.

3.3 Self-supervised speech: Wav2Vec 2.0 and HuBERT

Wav2Vec 2.0 learns by contrasting true masked latent speech targets against negatives. HuBERT learns by predicting cluster IDs for masked frames, then improves those clusters iteratively. Both reduce reliance on labeled transcripts and produce representations that downstream recognizers can fine-tune.

3.4 Music generation: Music Transformer, Jukebox, and codec-style models

Music has both local precision and long-range form: a motif may return after many bars. Music Transformer introduced efficient relative attention so notes can attend by distance, not only by absolute position. Jukebox uses hierarchical VQ-VAE audio codes and autoregressive transformers to generate music at multiple time scales.

Key equation — autoregressive acoustic tokens

$$p(z_{1:T})=\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(z_t\mid z_{<t},\text{artist},\text{lyrics},\text{level})$$

\(z_t\) is a discrete audio/codebook token. Conditioning variables such as artist, lyrics, or hierarchy level steer generation while the transformer predicts the next acoustic code.

✓ Check yourself — why is relative position especially natural for music?

A rhythm or motif is meaningful by interval: "repeat the phrase four beats later" is more transferable than "repeat whatever happened at absolute token 913." Relative attention encodes those distances directly.

4.Worked numeric micro-example — one second becomes patch tokens

Now compute the scale reduction by hand. This is the core reason spectrogram transformers are practical.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Take a 1-second mono clip at 16 kHz:

$$16{,}000\ \text{samples/sec}\times1\ \text{sec}=16{,}000\ \text{raw samples}$$

Use STFT window \(L=400\) samples (25 ms) and hop \(H=160\) samples (10 ms). A common practical convention pads/centers enough to get about one frame every 10 ms:

$$\text{frames}\approx \frac{1.0\ \text{sec}}{0.010\ \text{sec/frame}}=100\ \text{frames}$$

With 80 mel bins, the spectrogram has

$$80\times100=8{,}000\ \text{time-frequency pixels}.$$

Patchify with \(16\times16\) patches. Along frequency, \(\lceil 80/16\rceil=5\) patches. Along time, \(\lceil100/16\rceil=7\) patches after padding the last partial patch. So

$$T=5\times7=35\ \text{patch tokens}.$$

The contrast is the point: raw-sample attention would see \(N=16{,}000\); patch attention sees \(T=35\). Pairwise attention scores drop from

$$16{,}000^2=256{,}000{,}000\quad\text{to}\quad35^2=1{,}225.$$

✓ Check yourself — varying hop size: if hop becomes 80 samples, how many frames and patch tokens?

Hop 80 at 16 kHz is 5 ms, so 1 second gives about \(1/0.005=200\) frames. The mel image is \(80\times200\). With \(16\times16\) patches: \(\lceil80/16\rceil=5\), \(\lceil200/16\rceil=13\), so \(T=65\) patch tokens. Better time resolution, longer sequence.

What the trace reveals

The biggest length reduction happens before attention: waveform \((16000,)\) becomes mel frames \((80\times100)\), then patch tokens \((T\times d)\). The transformer is powerful because it receives events, not because it processes every sample.

6.Limits and design tradeoffs

Audio transformers are strong, not magic. Each representation chooses which errors become easy and which become painful.

6.1 Sequence length versus resolution

Smaller hops, smaller patches, or high-rate codec tokens preserve detail but increase \(N\). Larger patches or coarser codes shorten the sequence but blur transients, consonants, and fast notes. The right point depends on the task: tagging tolerates coarser tokens than waveform synthesis.

6.2 Streaming and latency

Full-context encoders can look into the future, which is convenient offline but unacceptable for live captions or hearing aids. Streaming systems use causal or chunked attention, accept latency, or revise outputs as more audio arrives.

6.3 Data and evaluation

Whisper-style supervision works because scale absorbs noise; smaller domains may not have that luxury. For generation, likelihood and spectrogram metrics do not fully capture human judgments of groove, naturalness, lyrics alignment, or long-range musical coherence.

Common misconception

"A better transformer fixes audio." Often the bottleneck is the tokenizer, the training data, or the evaluation target. If the representation deletes phase or merges two phonemes into one code, attention cannot recover that missing information downstream.

✓ Check yourself — choose a tokenizer for real-time wake-word detection

You would likely use short-window log-mel frames or a compact learned frontend, not raw samples or a giant non-causal encoder. The system needs low latency, robustness, and a small classification head more than perfect reconstructability.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
WaveformRaw sampled audio amplitude over time; faithful but extremely long at normal sampling rates.
Sampling rateHow many samples are recorded per second, e.g. 16 kHz means 16,000 samples each second.
STFTShort-time Fourier transform: Fourier analysis on many short overlapping windows.
Mel-spectrogramA spectrogram with frequency bins remapped to a perceptual mel scale and often log-compressed.
Patch tokenA flattened time-frequency tile projected into a transformer embedding, as in ViT/AST.
Vector quantizationMapping continuous latent vectors to nearest entries in a discrete codebook.
Wav2Vec 2.0A self-supervised speech model that predicts masked quantized latent targets from context.
HuBERTA self-supervised speech model that predicts hidden cluster assignments for masked frames.
ASTAudio Spectrogram Transformer; a ViT-style encoder over spectrogram patches for audio tagging/classification.
WhisperOpenAI's encoder-decoder speech recognition/translation model trained with large-scale weak supervision.
Relative attentionAttention that uses distances between tokens, useful when patterns recur at shifted positions, as in music.
Neural audio codecA learned compressor that turns waveform audio into compact discrete acoustic tokens and back.
