Mixture of Experts (MoE) & the Switch Transformer

MoE makes a transformer huge in parameters without making every token pay for every parameter: a learned router sends each token to a small expert subset.

🎓 Barret Zoph & Irwan Bello ⏱ ≈50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Dense transformers have a simple bargain: more parameters usually means more capability, but every token must run through the same giant stack. Sparsely-gated Mixture of Experts asks for a better bargain: can we store many specialized FFNs, activate only one or two per token, and keep the FLOPs per token roughly constant while the total parameter count climbs into the hundreds of billions or trillions?

Running analogy

Picture a hospital triage desk. Every patient first meets the same receptionist-router; then the router sends them to the right specialist clinic. The hospital can employ many specialists, but each patient only visits one or two, so the visit cost stays small while the hospital’s total knowledge grows.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

You will follow the note smoothly if these are fresh:

  • Softmax — turns logits into positive probabilities that sum to 1; the router uses it to score experts.
  • Transformer FFN / MLP block — the per-token dense sublayer after attention; MoE usually replaces this block with many alternative FFNs.
  • FLOPs per token — the approximate arithmetic cost to process one token; sparse routing tries to keep this near dense-model cost.
  • Parameter-vs-compute distinction — parameters are stored knowledge; compute is what you execute on this input. MoE increases the first much more than the second.

1.The scaling problem MoE tries to solve

Suppose a dense transformer FFN has one set of weights. Whether the token is France, mitochondria, or for-loop, it passes through the same FFN. Scaling the FFN gives every token more capacity, but every token also pays the larger compute bill.

1.1 Dense vs sparse compute

MoE keeps the shared attention pathway, but swaps some FFN layers for a bank of experts. Each expert is itself an FFN. The router decides which expert gets the current token.

Key equation — parameter count decoupled from active compute

For an FFN with model width \(d\) and hidden width \(d_{ff}\), a dense FFN has about \(2dd_{ff}\) weights and costs about \(2dd_{ff}\) multiply-adds per token. With \(N\) experts and top-\(k\) routing:

$$\text{stored FFN parameters} \approx N\cdot 2dd_{ff},\qquad \text{active FFN compute per token} \approx k\cdot 2dd_{ff}.$$

Here \(N\) is the number of specialist clinics and \(k\) is how many a token visits. The Switch Transformer sets \(k=1\), so adding experts mainly adds stored capacity, not per-token work.

Intuition

The lecture’s central move is not “make a smaller model.” It is “make a much larger model where each token activates a small block.” That is why MoE is sparse within a huge parameter matrix, not sparse in the pruning sense of deleting capacity.

✓ Check yourself — if \(N=64\) and \(k=1\), what changes when you double \(N\)?

The stored expert parameters roughly double. The FFN compute for one token is almost unchanged because it still runs one expert. Memory and communication pressure may rise, but per-token expert FLOPs do not double.

2.Router and experts: the core mechanism

Take one token representation \(x_t\in\mathbb{R}^d\). Before it reaches the expert bank, a small router computes a score for each expert. Those scores become probabilities, and the highest probability expert or experts receive the token.

2.1 Top-k routing and the Switch simplification

Shazeer et al. popularized sparsely-gated MoE with top-2-style routing; Switch Transformer simplifies the decision to top-1. The simplification matters: one destination per token reduces communication, makes capacity accounting easier, and was empirically strong at low capacity factors.

Key equation — routing

$$p(x_t)=\operatorname{softmax}(x_t W_r),\qquad S_t=\operatorname{TopK}(p(x_t),k),\qquad y_t=\sum_{i\in S_t}p_i(x_t)\,E_i(x_t).$$

\(W_r\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times N}\) is the router matrix, \(p_i(x_t)\) is the router probability for expert \(i\), \(E_i\) is expert \(i\)’s FFN, and \(S_t\) is the selected expert set. Switch uses \(k=1\), so \(y_t=p_jE_j(x_t)\) for the single winning expert \(j\).

Common misconception

“Sparse” here does not mean the model has fewer parameters. It often has vastly more. The sparsity is conditional activation: a token sees a small slice of a giant expert bank.

✓ Check yourself — why is top-1 cheaper to communicate than top-2?

Top-1 sends each token to one expert device and returns one expert output. Top-2 sends it to two destinations and must combine two results, which usually means more all-to-all traffic and more capacity slots.

3.Capacity factor, overflow, and load balancing

Routers are learned. Without guardrails, a router can discover that one expert looks best early in training and send too many tokens there. Real devices cannot accept an unbounded number of tokens per expert, so MoE needs capacity rules.

3.1 Expert capacity

For a batch with \(S=B\times T\) tokens and \(N\) experts, a common static capacity is:

Key equation — capacity factor

$$C=\left\lceil \frac{S}{N}\cdot \text{capacity\_factor}\right\rceil.$$

\(C\) is the number of token slots reserved per expert. If more than \(C\) tokens choose an expert, the extra tokens overflow: they may be dropped from the expert computation and passed through a residual path, depending on implementation.

3.2 Load-balancing loss

The lecture emphasizes that load balancing is not cosmetic. Balanced routing maps better to hardware because all experts finish at similar times and the static capacity slots are used efficiently.

Key equation — Switch auxiliary balance loss

$$L_{\text{aux}}=\alpha\,N\sum_{i=1}^{N} f_i P_i.$$

\(f_i\) is the fraction of tokens actually routed to expert \(i\), \(P_i\) is the average router probability mass assigned to expert \(i\), \(N\) is the number of experts, and \(\alpha\) controls the strength. The loss is small when traffic and probability mass are spread across experts instead of collapsing onto one clinic.

Balance without destroying specialization

The auxiliary loss is a nudge, not the main objective. If \(\alpha\) is too high, the router may care more about equal traffic than task quality. If it is too low, one expert can become overcrowded. The useful regime lets experts specialize while staying hardware-friendly.

✓ Check yourself — with \(S=16\), \(N=4\), and capacity factor \(1.25\), what is \(C\)?

\(C=\lceil(16/4)\times1.25\rceil=\lceil5\rceil=5\). Each expert gets five token slots, so the layer reserves \(4\times5=20\) expert slots for 16 tokens.

4.Worked numeric micro-example — one token, four experts

Now compute the Switch routing decision by hand. The token arrives at the router, which emits four logits for experts \(E_1\) through \(E_4\):

Step-by-step with real numbers

$$z=[2.0,\,1.0,\,0.1,\,0.5].$$

Softmax exponentiates and normalizes:

$$\exp(z)=[7.389,\,2.718,\,1.105,\,1.649],\qquad \sum_j\exp(z_j)=12.861.$$

$$p=\operatorname{softmax}(z)=[0.5745,\,0.2114,\,0.0859,\,0.1282].$$

Switch top-1 chooses \(E_1\) because \(0.5745\) is largest. If that expert returns vector \(E_1(x)\), the routed output is:

$$y=0.5745\,E_1(x).$$

For a tiny scalar illustration, if \(E_1(x)=10\), then \(y=5.745\). In the real model, \(E_1(x)\) is a \(d\)-dimensional vector and the same scalar gate multiplies the vector.

✓ Check yourself — if expert 2’s logit becomes \(2.4\), who wins?

The logits are \([2.0,2.4,0.1,0.5]\). The exponentials are approximately \([7.389,11.023,1.105,1.649]\), sum \(21.166\), so the probabilities are \([0.349,0.521,0.052,0.078]\). Expert \(E_2\) wins, and Switch outputs \(0.521\,E_2(x)\).

5.End-to-end trace — one batch through a Switch layer

Follow a batch through a single MoE replacement for the FFN block. Let \(B=2\), \(T=4\), model width \(d=8\), experts \(N=4\), and capacity factor \(1.0\). Then \(S=8\) tokens and \(C=\lceil8/4\rceil=2\) token slots per expert.

StepWhat happensShape / state
0 · inputTransformer block has token representations after attention and residual normalization.input: B×T×d = 2×4×8
1 · flatten tokensThe router treats the batch as \(S=B\times T\) token vectors.tokens: S×d = 8×8
2 · router logitsMultiply by router weights to score each expert.logits: S×N = 8×4
3 · softmax + top-1Convert scores to probabilities and choose the largest expert per token.assignments: 8 expert IDs + 8 gate probs
4 · dispatch with capacityTokens are grouped by expert. With \(C=2\), the third token assigned to the same expert overflows here.expert input buffers: N×C×d = 4×2×8
5 · expert FFNEach expert runs its own FFN on the tokens in its buffer; empty slots are masked.expert outputs: 4×2×8
6 · combineEach output is multiplied by its gate probability and placed back in the original token order.combined tokens: S×d = 8×8
7 · reshape outputThe layer returns to the transformer block shape.output: B×T×d = 2×4×8
What the trace reveals

The dynamic part is the assignment; the tensors sent to hardware are made static with capacity slots. That is why token dropping, padding, and all-to-all dispatch are not side details — they are the bridge from a learned router to real accelerators.

✓ Check yourself — where exactly can token dropping happen in the trace?

At step 4, dispatch with capacity. Once an expert’s \(C\) slots are full, later tokens assigned to that expert cannot enter its FFN buffer unless the implementation reroutes them or increases capacity.

6.Switch Transformer specifics, systems costs, and limits

Switch Transformer is a practical recipe, not only an architectural slogan: use top-1 routing, replace some FFN layers with sparse layers, balance expert loads, keep router math stable, and distribute experts across devices.

6.1 What Switch changes

  • Top-1 routing: one expert per token, simpler than top-2 and often faster at low capacity factors.
  • Capacity factor: controls the overflow-vs-cost tradeoff; lower values reduce communication and padding but risk dropping tokens.
  • Expert parallelism: different devices hold different experts, so parameter count scales with device count.
  • Selective precision: much of the model can use bfloat16, while router softmax computations are cast to float32 for stability because exponentials amplify rounding errors.
  • Scale: the paper reports T5-based sparse models up to about 1.6T parameters, with strong pretraining speedups such as up to roughly 7× over a T5-base dense baseline in the discussed setup.

6.2 The systems angle

Expert parallelism is attractive because each device can own different parameters. But if a token on device 0 chooses an expert on device 3, the activation must cross the network. That all-to-all communication can dominate runtime as expert counts grow. Sharding plans therefore co-design model dimensions, expert placement, data parallelism, and model parallelism.

6.3 Limitations and honest tradeoffs

Common failure modes
  • Training instability: sparse routing and low precision can diverge unless the router is handled carefully.
  • Memory footprint: inactive experts still must live somewhere; MoE is weak when you are strictly parameter-memory constrained.
  • Fine-tuning and overfitting: many parameters can overfit small downstream tasks; the lecture discusses stronger expert dropout and task-specific choices about auxiliary loss.
  • Communication overhead: all-to-all traffic can erase compute savings if routing or sharding is poorly matched to hardware.
  • Reasoning vs knowledge tradeoff: the lecture reports sparse models can shine on knowledge-heavy tasks while very sparse, low-FLOP models may lag dense models on some reasoning-heavy fine-tuning comparisons.
Design rule of thumb

MoE is most compelling when you want high-throughput pretraining or serving, have enough devices to store experts, and can afford the network traffic. If the goal is the best model that fits on one tiny device, dense or distilled dense models may be better.

✓ Check yourself — why did “no token left behind” not automatically help?

If the router strongly wanted expert 1, sending an overflow token to its second-best expert may apply the wrong specialist. The lecture notes that rerouting dropped tokens did not empirically improve performance and could hurt, suggesting that “some expert computation” is not always better than the chosen expert being unavailable.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Mixture of Experts (MoE)A layer with many expert subnetworks plus a router that activates only a small subset for each token.
ExpertAn independent FFN/MLP block, usually replacing a transformer feed-forward sublayer.
Router / gateA learned small network that maps a token representation to probabilities over experts.
Top-k routingSending a token to the \(k\) experts with highest router probabilities.
Switch routingTop-1 routing: each token is sent to a single expert.
Capacity factorA multiplier that sets how many token slots each expert reserves for a batch.
Token dropping / overflowWhat happens when more tokens choose an expert than its capacity allows.
Load-balancing lossAn auxiliary training term that discourages all tokens from using the same expert.
Expert parallelismDistributing different experts across different devices.
All-to-all communicationThe distributed exchange that sends token activations to selected expert devices and returns outputs.
Selective precisionUsing low precision for most computation while keeping numerically sensitive router operations in float32.
