Common-Sense Reasoning in Language Models

Yejin Choi's lecture shows why fluent transformers still need explicit ways to surface, check, distill, and reason over the unstated everyday knowledge humans use effortlessly.

🎓 Yejin Choi ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A child knows that a dropped glass may break, a fridge left open can spoil food, and one person cannot be in two places at once. Most of this knowledge is rarely written down because it is too obvious to mention. That is why common sense is the dark matter of intelligence: mostly invisible in text, yet shaping almost every interpretation and action.

Running analogy

Think of language as the visible script of a play and common sense as the backstage crew. The script contains the lines; the crew silently checks props, entrances, timing, and what would be impossible. A language model trained on scripts can sound convincing while missing the backstage directions everyone assumed.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before we ask what the model really knows.

  • Language model and next-token generation — a transformer estimates the next token from context; here that mechanism can also emit explanations or commonsense inferences.
  • Knowledge graph — knowledge as nodes plus labeled edges; ATOMIC connects events to likely intents, effects, reactions, needs, and blockers.
  • Prompting / few-shot learning — steer a pretrained model at inference time with instructions or examples; many reasoning methods below are prompt-time algorithms.
  • Softmax — turns raw scores into probabilities that sum to 1; we use it to rank generated inferences.

1.The dark matter problem

Choi opens with a Winograd-style sentence: "The trophy does not fit in the brown suitcase because it is too big." Humans resolve it as the trophy. But after a small paraphrase, a model may produce a confident contradiction. The lesson is not that LLMs know nothing; it is that fluency is not stable, inspectable world knowledge.

1.1 Benchmarks and reporting bias

Benchmarks probe different parts of the hidden backstage crew. They are difficult because of reporting bias: people write "the vase shattered," but rarely state the rule "fragile things can break when dropped."

BenchmarkWhat it probesWhy it is tricky
CommonsenseQAQuestion answering grounded in commonsense relations.The ordinary answer is not always the most lexical association.
SWAG / HellaSwagLikely continuations of everyday situations.Wrong endings are grammatical but physically or socially implausible.
Winograd SchemaPronoun resolution using world knowledge.Tiny wording changes can flip the referent.
PIQAPhysical interaction and affordances.Needs knowledge about spilling, fitting, cutting, bending, and tool use.
Social IQaSocial intent, reaction, and effect.Requires tacit social scripts such as gratitude, embarrassment, apology, and trust.
Common misconception

Wrong model: "A big LLM already has full common sense." Better model: it has many fragments of common sense, but those fragments can be brittle under paraphrase, negation, counterfactuals, and new contexts.

✓ Check yourself — invent a reporting-bias fact

Example: "A full cup spills if tipped over." It is useful for prediction but rarely written unless something unusual happens.

2.Knowledge graphs: ATOMIC and COMET

For the event PersonX gets PersonX's car repaired, humans infer a mechanic, a bill, perhaps needing a ride, and a blocker if the car is totaled. ATOMIC makes these if-then inferences explicit; COMET learns to generate new ones.

2.1 ATOMIC-style if-then edges

ATOMIC is a commonsense knowledge graph over typed event relations: intents, effects, reactions, needs, hindrances, affordances, and before/after conditions. It includes social knowledge and physical/entity-centric knowledge.

COMET is a transformer trained on ATOMIC/ConceptNet-style data. Given an event and a relation type, it generates plausible inference text. That is generation, not merely lookup: it can propose a backstage direction not already stored as an exact edge.

Key equation — relation-conditioned generation

$$p_\theta(y\mid e,r)=\prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta\!\left(y_t\mid y_{<t}, e, r\right)$$

Here, \(e\) is the event, \(r\) is a relation such as xIntent, and \(y_1,\ldots,y_T\) are the generated inference tokens.

✓ Check yourself — which relation answers "How does PersonY feel?"

Use an object-side reaction relation such as oReact. For the gift event, plausible outputs include "grateful" or "appreciated."

3.Symbolic knowledge distillation

Human crowdsourcing built early ATOMIC, but coverage is expensive. West et al.'s move is to use a large LM as a noisy author of symbolic commonsense edges, filter aggressively with a critic, then train a smaller, cleaner commonsense model.

The critic need not be perfect. If the teacher can generate millions of candidates, a strict critic can throw away many true edges and still leave a large, cleaner dataset.

Key equation — distilling sampled symbolic outputs

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}}(\theta)=-\mathbb{E}_{y\sim q_T(\cdot\mid x)}\left[\log p_\theta(y\mid x)\right]\quad\text{with}\quad c(x,y)\ge\tau$$

The input \(x\) is the event-relation prompt, \(q_T\) is the teacher distribution, \(p_\theta\) is the student, and \(c(x,y)\ge\tau\) means the critic accepts the edge.

Why a smaller model can improve

The student is not copying every habit of the teacher. It studies a curated syllabus of filtered if-then knowledge, so it can become a better backstage crew for this specific job.

✓ Check yourself — why can a noisy teacher help?

Because the pipeline changes quantity and quality. The teacher supplies scale; the critic supplies selectivity; the student specializes on the resulting commonsense task.

4.Worked example — one ATOMIC inference by hand

Event: PersonX gives PersonY a gift. COMET-style relation outputs might be xIntent → to be kind, xReact → happy, oReact → grateful, and xEffect → spends money. For xIntent, suppose candidate scores are:

  • to be kind: \(2.0\)
  • to apologize: \(1.0\)
  • to be mean: \(-1.0\)
Step-by-step with real numbers — softmax ranking

$$P_i=\frac{e^{s_i}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}}$$

The exponentials are \(e^{2.0}=7.39\), \(e^{1.0}=2.72\), and \(e^{-1.0}=0.37\). The denominator is \(7.39+2.72+0.37=10.48\).

$$P(\text{kind})=\frac{7.39}{10.48}=0.71,\quad P(\text{apologize})=0.26,\quad P(\text{mean})=0.04$$

The likely xIntent is therefore to be kind. This is a ranked commonsense guess, not a logical proof.

✓ Check yourself — new event: PersonX forgets PersonY's birthday

Plausible edges: xReact → sorry, oReact → hurt, xEffect → apologizes. If xReact scores are sorry \(1.5\), proud \(-0.5\), confused \(0.0\), softmax is about \(0.74,0.10,0.16\), so "sorry" wins.

5.End-to-end trace — one commonsense query

Follow one query through a COMET/prompting pipeline. The same event moves from text, to tokens, to hidden states, to ranked candidate knowledge.

StepWhat happensOutput
0 · query eventAsk for the intent behind PersonX gives PersonY a gift.event = gift-giving; relation = xIntent
1 · tokenizeThe event and relation become model tokens.[PersonX, gives, PersonY, a, gift, xIntent]
2 · transformerThe model conditions on who did what to whom and on the requested relation.hidden states encode event + relation
3 · generate inference tokensAutoregressive decoding proposes "to be kind," "to apologize," and other strings.candidate text + token scores
4 · score and rank candidatesSoftmax or beam scores rank outputs; a critic/verifier can filter implausible edges.kind 0.71; apologize 0.26; mean 0.04
5 · chosen inferenceThe top plausible edge is returned.xIntent(PersonX, to be kind)
Generation versus retrieval

Retrieval asks, "Is this exact edge already in the graph?" Generation asks, "Given this event and relation, what edge should exist?" COMET matters because everyday life has too many possible stage directions to prewrite them all.

6.Reasoning at inference time

Knowledge graphs improve memory; prompt-time methods try to make the model reason more consistently. Choi's examples show GPT-3 sometimes states a fact correctly, then contradicts it under a true/false or negated version.

6.1 Maieutic prompting

Maieutic prompting borrows the Socratic method: ask the model to explain why an answer might be true and why it might be false, recursively test explanations and negations, then choose labels that maximize consistency across the graph.

Key equation — consistency objective

$$\hat{z}=\arg\max_z\left(\sum_i b_i z_i+\sum_{i<j}w_{ij}\mathbf{1}[z_i=z_j]\right)$$

\(z_i\) is a truth label for node \(i\), \(b_i\) is its belief score, and \(w_{ij}\) rewards pairwise consistency. A local answer can be overridden by graph-level coherence.

MethodWhat it addsFailure it targets
Chain-of-thoughtExpose intermediate reasoning.Hidden leaps in the answer.
Self-consistencySample many chains and vote.One brittle sampled path.
Maieutic promptingExplain both sides and enforce consistency.Self-contradictory rationales.
Verifier / criticFilter generated claims with symbolic or learned checks.Fluent hallucinated "facts."
✓ Check yourself — why not trust the first chain-of-thought?

A single chain is one sampled story. Self-consistency, critics, and maieutic graphs ask whether the answer survives alternative paths, negation, and contradiction checks.

7.What LLMs still get wrong

The lecture is not anti-LLM. It is anti-overclaim. Emergent abilities are impressive, but common sense is full of defeasible rules: usually true, context-sensitive, and easy to break under adversarial wording.

Common failure modes
  • Brittleness: small paraphrases can flip an answer.
  • Negation: a model may know a fact but mishandle its negated form.
  • Physical and temporal reasoning: fitting, spilling, breaking, before/after, and affordances remain fragile.
  • Hallucinated facts: plausible-sounding completions are not guaranteed true.
  • Inconsistency: two outputs from the same model may conflict.

Choi's later moral/social examples make the point sharper: language systems already make outputs with social consequences. The safer model is not "the system has common sense," but "it has fragments that need elicitation, filtering, and verification."

✓ Check yourself — classify the failure

If a model says "stabbing someone with a cheeseburger is physically dangerous," it misses physical affordance knowledge. If it says "stabbing someone over a cheeseburger is harmless," it misses linguistic and social context: the default omitted instrument for stabbing is a knife.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Common senseBasic practical knowledge and reasoning about everyday situations, shared by many people but full of contextual exceptions.
Reporting biasThe omission of obvious facts from text because writers assume readers already know them.
ATOMICA commonsense knowledge graph of if-then event relations.
COMETA transformer that generates commonsense graph edges from an event and relation type.
Symbolic knowledge distillationUsing a large LM to generate symbolic knowledge, filtering it, and training a smaller specialized model on the result.
Maieutic promptingRecursive explanation prompting plus consistency checking over an explanation graph.
Neuro-symbolic reasoningCombining neural language models with symbolic structures such as graphs, solvers, verifiers, or critics.
