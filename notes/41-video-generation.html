Transformers for Video Generation

Andrew Brown explains how modern text-to-video systems turn prompts into coherent moving worlds by combining compressed video latents, flow/diffusion objectives, and scaled transformers.

🎓 Andrew Brown (Meta)⏱ ~50 min▶ Watch
Why this matters

Image generation asks, “Can I make one plausible still?” Video generation asks, “Can I make many plausible stills that agree about identity, motion, lighting, camera movement, and physics over time?” The hard part is not only the huge token count: it is temporal coherence—the ghost should still be the same ghost when it moves, and its reflection should move with it.

Running analogy — storyboarding a flipbook

Think of the model as a film student making a flipbook. A weak student draws each page independently: page 12 has a sloth, page 13 has a vaguely different sloth. A strong student plans the whole flipbook as one spacetime object, so character identity, motion, and cause/effect carry across pages.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

You will follow this note smoothly if these are fresh:

  • Diffusion / flow matching — train a network to move noisy data back toward clean data; this note uses the same denoising idea as Note 40 on DiT.
  • ViT patchify — split an image into patches, embed each patch as a token, then use a transformer; video extends Note 4 on ViT from 2D patches to 3D tubelets.
  • Attention is quadratic — full self-attention over \(N\) tokens compares every token to every other token, costing \(O(N^2)\).
  • VAE / latent space — an encoder compresses pixels into a smaller latent; a decoder later turns generated latents back into pixels.
  • Text conditioning — text features enter the video model through cross-attention or similar conditioning, so the prompt guides denoising without being noised itself.

1.Video is images plus time

A single generated frame can hide many sins. In a video, every frame checks the previous frame: did the car keep its shape? did the camera obey the same scene geometry? did the sloth on the donut float remain the same sloth?

1.1 Time changes the problem

Brown's lecture traces the jump from early 2022 text-to-video systems to 2024 systems such as Movie Gen: the field moved from specialized CNN/U-Net-style architectures toward scaled transformers. The reason is architectural unification: once a modality is turned into a token sequence, the same scaling playbook used for language can be reused for media.

Common misconception — “just generate frames independently”

If each frame is generated alone, the model has no strong reason to preserve identity, motion direction, object permanence, or physical interactions. You may get 16 nice images, but not one coherent clip. Video generation is a joint spacetime problem, not repeated image generation.

Key equation — the size of the raw video object

$$x_{\text{video}} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times H \times W \times C}$$

Here \(T\) is frames, \(H\) and \(W\) are frame height and width, and \(C\) is channels. Images have \(H\times W\times C\); video multiplies that by time.

✓ Check yourself — why does a beautiful per-frame model still flicker?

Because the model can choose slightly different identities, textures, poses, or lighting in each frame. Coherence requires the random choices for one frame to be constrained by neighboring frames.

2.Spacetime patches become transformer tokens

Instead of treating a video as millions of unrelated pixels, modern systems cut it into small 3D chunks: a little area of space over a short duration. These spacetime patches, or tubelets, become tokens.

2.1 Tubelets, not pixels

A tubelet might cover \(2\) frames, \(16\) pixels of height, and \(16\) pixels of width. Flatten it, project it into a vector of width \(d\), and it can enter a transformer exactly as a word embedding or ViT patch embedding would.

Key equation — token count after spacetime patching

$$N = \frac{T}{\Delta t}\,\frac{H}{p}\,\frac{W}{p}$$

\(\Delta t\) is temporal patch size, \(p\) is spatial patch size, and \(N\) is video tokens before adding text conditioning.

✓ Check yourself — what happens to tokens if you double the duration?

If resolution and patch sizes stay fixed, \(T\) doubles, so \(N\) doubles. Full attention then grows by roughly four times because it scales as \(N^2\).

3.Latent video diffusion + a Diffusion Transformer

Brown stresses that Movie Gen does not model RGB pixels directly. It first compresses video with a temporal autoencoder, then learns a generative model over the compressed latent. That is why large videos become computationally feasible.

3.1 Movie Gen's recipe

In Movie Gen, a temporal autoencoder compresses space and time; Brown reports \(8\times\) compression in height, width, and time. A 16-second, 768×768, 16 FPS clip that would be enormous in pixel space becomes about 73K latent tokens after compression and patching. The denoiser is a transformer with three adaptations to a Llama-style architecture: text cross-attention, timestep conditioning via adaptive layer norm, and bidirectional self-attention instead of causal masking.

Key equation — diffusion-style noise prediction

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{diff}} = \mathbb{E}_{z_0,\epsilon,t,c}\left[\left\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(z_t,t,c)\right\|_2^2\right],\qquad z_t = \alpha_t z_0 + \sigma_t\epsilon$$

\(z_0\) is the clean video latent, \(\epsilon\) is Gaussian noise, \(t\) is the noise level, \(c\) is text conditioning, and \(\epsilon_\theta\) is the transformer denoiser. Movie Gen uses the closely related flow matching objective, predicting a velocity from noisy latent toward data latent.

Movie Gen detail — flow matching target

$$x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1, \qquad \mathcal{L}_{\text{flow}} = \mathbb{E}\left[\left\|u_\theta(x_t,t,c) - (x_1-x_0)\right\|_2^2\right]$$

Here \(x_0\) is noise, \(x_1\) is a clean latent sample, and \(u_\theta\) predicts the velocity along the path from noise to data.

What the transcript adds

Brown's key systems claim is scale: Movie Gen is a 30B-parameter foundation model trained on roughly 100M licensed videos and 1B images. Its data pipeline filters visual quality, motion quality, scene changes, duplicates, and concept imbalance; captions are generated by a video-conditioned Llama 3 captioner.

✓ Check yourself — where does the prompt enter if the input is pure noise?

The noisy video latent is the main input, but text embeddings enter through cross-attention in every denoising step. The prompt is clean conditioning information, not part of the noised video latent.

4.Cost, temporal consistency, and scale

The same transformer simplicity that makes scaling attractive also makes the cost scary. Full 3D attention over every spacetime token is expensive, so practical systems use compression, patching, parallelism, and often attention patterns that exploit video structure.

4.1 Factorized spatial + temporal attention

Full attention lets every token compare with every other token. Factorized attention breaks the question into two passes: first “what is happening inside this frame or temporal group?” then “how does this location evolve through time?” This keeps much of the flipbook coherence while cutting the \(O(N^2)\) blowup.

Key equation — full vs factorized attention scale

$$\text{full} \approx (S\tau)^2, \qquad \text{factorized} \approx \tau S^2 + S\tau^2$$

\(S\) is spatial tokens per frame or temporal group, and \(\tau\) is the number of temporal groups. Full attention compares all \(S\tau\) tokens together; factorized attention compares space and time separately.

How coherence emerges

Coherence comes from several pressures at once: joint spacetime tokens expose nearby frames to each other; text conditioning anchors the scene; denoising repeatedly revises the whole clip; and scale plus data teaches regularities such as object permanence, reflections, and plausible motion. Brown's main claim is not that the architecture hard-codes physics, but that large transformers can learn much of it from large clean video corpora.

Limitations to keep honest

Video generation is not solved. Brown shows a car-crash prompt where cars split or implode: complex physics, long-range planning, and detailed multi-step prompts still fail. Long videos hit sequence-length limits; evaluation remains hard, so teams rely heavily on human preference studies alongside metrics such as FVD.

✓ Check yourself — why can more data help a transformer more than a hand-built video prior?

At small scale, a prior such as convolutional locality can help. At large scale, the transformer can learn many useful regularities while remaining flexible, and the infrastructure/scaling laws for transformers are well understood.

5.Worked example — why video is expensive

Take the required micro-case: a 16-frame clip at 256×256 resolution. Use spatial patches of 16×16 pixels and temporal patches of 2 frames.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Spatial tokens per frame-group:

$$S = \left(\frac{256}{16}\right)^2 = 16^2 = 256$$

Temporal groups:

$$\tau = \frac{16}{2}=8$$

Total spacetime tokens:

$$N = S\tau = 256\times 8 = 2048$$

Full self-attention scores:

$$N^2 = 2048^2 = 4{,}194{,}304 \approx 4.2\text{M scores}$$

Factorized spatial + temporal attention:

$$\tau S^2 + S\tau^2 = 8\cdot256^2 + 256\cdot8^2 = 524{,}288 + 16{,}384 = 540{,}672$$

So this simple factorization is about \(4{,}194{,}304 / 540{,}672 \approx 7.8\times\) fewer attention scores before considering heads, layers, or batch size.

✓ Check yourself — if the clip doubles to 32 frames, what changes?

Temporal groups become \(\tau=32/2=16\), so \(N=256\times16=4096\). Full attention becomes \(4096^2=16{,}777{,}216\) scores. Factorized attention becomes \(16\cdot256^2 + 256\cdot16^2 = 1{,}048{,}576 + 65{,}536 = 1{,}114{,}112\) scores.

6.End-to-end trace — text prompt to video

Follow one prompt through a latent video diffusion/flow transformer pipeline. Shapes are schematic; real systems choose compression, patch sizes, and model width based on compute budgets.

Why joint spacetime modeling gives coherence

The transformer is not drawing frame 1, then forgetting it before drawing frame 2. It repeatedly updates a shared spacetime latent, so tokens for nearby places and times can coordinate. That shared workspace is what lets identity, motion, reflections, and camera geometry become properties of the whole clip.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Spacetime patch / tubeletA small 3D chunk of video covering a patch of pixels across a few frames; used as a transformer token.
Temporal coherenceThe property that objects, motion, lighting, and identity remain consistent across frames.
Latent videoA compressed representation of a video produced by an encoder; cheaper to model than RGB pixels.
Temporal autoencoder / video VAEAn encoder-decoder model that compresses and reconstructs video across both space and time.
Diffusion Transformer (DiT)A transformer used as the denoising network in a diffusion model, often with timestep conditioning.
Flow matchingA diffusion-like objective that trains a model to predict the velocity moving noise toward data.
Cross-attentionA mechanism where video tokens attend to text embeddings, letting the prompt guide generation.
FVDFréchet Video Distance, an automatic metric comparing distributions of generated and real videos; useful but incomplete.
