Distinct Modes of Generalization from Parameters and Context

Andrew Lampinen’s core message: language models can store the same information in weights or in context, but those two stores do not generalize in the same way.

🎓 Andrew Lampinen · Anthropic ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Suppose your system answers from memory that “Sam is Acme’s CEO,” but the user supplied a fresh document saying “Dana is Acme’s CEO.” Should the model trust its parametric knowledge or the in-context evidence? Lampinen’s talk shows that this is not merely a product-design question. It reflects two genuinely different computational modes: knowledge compressed into weights during training, and knowledge temporarily available in the prompt at inference.

This distinction matters for RAG, fine-tuning, personalization, factual updates, and reasoning. Retrieval is not only a way to add missing facts; it can make already-seen facts usable in a more flexible form. See also Note 3 on in-context learning and Note 25 on retrieval-augmented LMs.

Running analogy

Think of a model as a lawyer with two resources. The weights are the lawyer’s long-trained legal instincts: fast, compressed, and broad, but sometimes tied to the exact way precedents were learned. The context window is the case file on the desk: limited in size, but inspectable and reusable for the exact question today. The lecture asks when the lawyer reasons from instinct, when from the case file, and how to combine both.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

These are the only moving parts you need before the results.

  • Parameters / weights — learned numbers updated by gradient descent; here they are the model’s compressed long-term store of facts, skills, and statistical structure.
  • Prompt / context — tokens supplied at inference; here they act like a temporary working file that attention can directly inspect.
  • In-context learning — adapting behavior from examples or facts in the prompt without changing weights; refresh via Note 3.
  • Induction heads — attention circuits that copy or continue patterns from earlier tokens; a mechanistic foothold for in-context learning, linked to Note 8.
  • Softmax over logits — candidate answers receive scores; \\(p_i=e^{z_i}/\sum_j e^{z_j}\\) turns those scores into probabilities.
  • RAG — retrieval-augmented generation supplies external documents in context; in this lecture, retrieval also repairs some failures of parametric generalization (Note 25).

1.Two ways an LLM “knows”

Start with one made-up fact: Daphne Barrington directed A Journey Through Time. A model can encounter that sentence during fine-tuning, so the fact becomes part of the weights. Or the sentence can appear in the prompt right before the question. The surprising result is that these two exposures can lead to very different answers when the question changes direction.

1.1 The same fact, two stores

Parametric learning compresses many documents into reusable parameters. It is fast at test time and supports broad statistical generalization, but it can be hard to update and can be overly tied to how a fact was phrased during training. In-context learning keeps the fact as explicit tokens. It is flexible and immediately updatable, but bounded by context length and by the model’s ability to attend to the right tokens.

Key equation — two sources combine at decoding

$$z(a \mid x,C) \approx z_{\text{param}}(a \mid x;\theta) + z_{\text{ctx}}(a \mid x,C;\theta)$$

Here, \\(a\\) is a candidate answer, \\(x\\) is the question, \\(C\\) is the prompt context, and \\(\theta\\) are the learned weights. This is a schematic, not a literal decomposition from the paper: it helps us track how a model may add pressure from long-term memory and from evidence in the current context.

Common misconception

“If the fact is in the model, the model can use it in every logically equivalent way.” Lampinen’s evidence says no. A causal next-token model may know how to complete “Daphne Barrington directed …” while still failing “Who directed A Journey Through Time?” if the reverse direction was not made explicit or cued correctly.

✓ Check yourself — which store is easier to update today?

Context. You can paste a new fact or retrieved document into the prompt immediately. Updating weights requires training or fine-tuning and risks interference with other behavior.

2.The generalization gap: same data, different behavior

The talk’s central controlled comparison is: put a dataset into the model’s parameters via fine-tuning, or put the whole dataset into the context of a long-context model. Then ask for generalizations that are implied by the data but not necessarily written in the tested form.

2.1 Reversals, syllogisms, and codebooks

The most memorable case is the reversal curse. In one setup, a pre-trained model is at chance on held-out reversals; fine-tuning on only the forward relation can even make reversal accuracy slightly worse than chance; placing the full dataset in context yields about 99% reversal accuracy in Lampinen’s replication. The fact is available in both cases, but it is more flexibly usable when explicit in context.

The same theme appears in syllogistic generalization. From statements like “all zamp are snaff” and “no snaff are plusk,” the model should infer “no zamp are plusk.” Fine-tuned models show only modest above-chance latent generalization, while in-context models can use the statements more flexibly. In scratch-trained toy models, Lampinen’s team held out 1% of reversals: the model remembered trained relations and could reverse a relation if it was placed in context, yet got 0% on the held-out parametric reversals. Codebook tasks show a related failure: the model recalls pieces, and uses pieces flexibly in context, but struggles to compose the latent implication from weights alone.

Key equation — explicit statements can imply latent facts

$$\text{All }X\subseteq Y,\quad Y\cap Z=\varnothing \quad \Rightarrow \quad X\cap Z=\varnothing$$

The left side is explicit in the documents. The right side is latent: true given the documents, but not necessarily written. The lecture’s question is whether the model can recover that latent fact from weights, context, retrieval, or reasoning traces.

✓ Check yourself — why use nonsense nouns like “zamp” and “plusk”?

They block the model from leaning on real-world semantic associations. If the model succeeds, it is more likely using the provided structure rather than memorized facts about familiar categories.

3.Why context can be more flexible

Lampinen’s interpretation is not “weights are bad, context is good.” It is more precise: weights are excellent at consolidating statistical structure across many documents, while context preserves specific episodes in richer detail. Those have different strengths.

3.1 Explicit vs latent information

Training documents often contain more than their literal surface. “X is Y’s parent” explicitly gives one direction; it latently implies “Y is X’s child.” Two syllogism premises explicitly give two statements; they latently imply a conclusion. A model trained by next-token prediction may become very good at the explicit continuation while failing to expose all latent implications when asked from a new angle.

At the same time, parametric learning can generalize through statistics. If training data contains “eagles fly,” “pigeons are birds,” “pigeons have wings,” “hawks fly,” and many similar co-occurrences, a model may infer “eagles have wings” without doing a crisp logical syllogism. That is a legitimate statistical shortcut in expectation, but it can fail on exceptions such as penguins.

Mechanistic intuition

Induction-style circuits and attention can reuse patterns that are literally present in the current sequence: “I saw a relation earlier; now I can copy, reverse, or compose it for this query.” Weights supply the procedures for doing that. But when the relation is only compressed into weights, the model may not retrieve the exact episode in the form the query requires.

✓ Check yourself — why is “compression” both a feature and a bug?

It is a feature because compression extracts reusable statistical structure across many documents. It is a bug when the compressed representation drops details needed for a later, unanticipated generalization.

4.Bridging the gap: three paths from the talk

The second half of the talk asks how to make latent information usable when plain parametric learning fails. Lampinen organizes the solutions by when compute is spent: before deployment, during retrieval, or during test-time reasoning.

4.1 Augment, retrieve, or think

Train-time augmentation uses in-context learning to generate reasoning traces from the dataset: fill in reversals, connect documents, and make latent conclusions explicit. Those traces are then distilled back into the weights by fine-tuning. This can match in-context performance on reversal-like tasks and even exceed it on syllogisms because many sampled traces create more chances to expose the right link.

Test-time episodic retrieval pulls relevant training episodes back into context. Lampinen’s experiments used an oracle memory with perfect recall but imperfect precision: it always includes something useful plus distractors. Even with distractors, retrieval greatly improves reversal and codebook latent tests because the model can reason over the specific retrieved facts as context.

RL-trained test-time thinking asks the model to regenerate useful facts in its chain of thought. It helps on several structures and can generalize from one dataset to another, but reversals remain hard: to reverse “X contains Y,” the model often has to find the forward fact first, which becomes an inefficient search at scale.

Key equation — the augmentation idea

$$D_{\text{aug}}=D_{\text{train}}\cup \{\text{ICL}(D_{\text{train}}, d_i)\;:\; d_i\in D_{\text{train}}\}$$

Read this as: start with the original training documents \\(D_{\text{train}}\\); ask the model, with the dataset in context, to elaborate each document \\(d_i\\); add those generated implications to form an augmented dataset \\(D_{\text{aug}}\\). The new text does not create magical new information; it makes already-implied information explicit and usable.

Common misconception

“Synthetic augmentation discovers brand-new facts.” The lecture’s stronger claim is subtler: augmentation can extract usable information that was already latent in the data, like a syllogism conclusion implied by premises. This is why the “data processing inequality” objection is not the whole story under computational constraints.

✓ Check yourself — when would retrieval beat fine-tuning?

When the needed information is fresh, rare, user-specific, or useful in an unanticipated way. Retrieval can put the original episode into context without changing the whole model.

5.Worked micro-example (#2) — a knowledge conflict by hand

Now make the routing problem numerical. The prompt says: “The CEO of Acme is Dana.” The model’s weights, from older training data, encode a prior leaning toward Sam. We will represent the two candidate answers by logits: one logit from context-supported evidence, one from parametric prior.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Candidate answers: \\(a_c=\\) Dana (context-supported) and \\(a_p=\\) Sam (parametric prior).

Strong prompt evidence gives logits

$$z_{\text{Dana}}=3.0,\qquad z_{\text{Sam}}=2.0.$$

Softmax probabilities are

$$p(\text{Dana})=\frac{e^{3.0}}{e^{3.0}+e^{2.0}}=\frac{20.09}{20.09+7.39}=\frac{20.09}{27.48}\approx 0.73,$$

$$p(\text{Sam})=\frac{e^{2.0}}{e^{3.0}+e^{2.0}}=\frac{7.39}{27.48}\approx 0.27.$$

So the model favors the in-context answer Dana. If the context is weak or buried, its contribution might drop to \\(z_{\text{Dana}}=1.5\\) while the parametric prior remains \\(z_{\text{Sam}}=2.0\\):

$$p(\text{Dana})=\frac{e^{1.5}}{e^{1.5}+e^{2.0}}=\frac{4.48}{4.48+7.39}=\frac{4.48}{11.87}\approx 0.38,$$

and now Sam wins with probability \\(0.62\\). The arithmetic captures the qualitative point: the model is not guaranteed to obey context; it depends on how strongly context evidence is represented and how strong the weight-based prior is.

✓ Check yourself — what happens when context is absent?

If Dana has no context support, imagine logits \\(z_{\text{Dana}}=0.0\\), \\(z_{\text{Sam}}=2.0\\). Then \\(p(\text{Sam})=e^2/(e^2+e^0)=7.39/(7.39+1)=0.88\\). The model mostly follows its parametric prior.

✓ Check yourself — how could a stronger prompt flip the answer more decisively?

Raise the context-supported logit, for example by providing a clear retrieved source: \\(z_{\text{Dana}}=4\\), \\(z_{\text{Sam}}=2\\). Then \\(p(\text{Dana})=e^4/(e^4+e^2)=54.60/(54.60+7.39)=0.88\\).

6.End-to-end trace (#4) — answer one query from both paths

Trace the concrete query: “Who is the CEO of Acme?” The prompt includes one sentence: The CEO of Acme is Dana. The weights contain older statistical memory favoring Sam.

StepParametric pathIn-context pathnote
0 · queryWeights encode old associations between Acme ↔ CEO ↔ Sam.The current prompt contains explicit tokens CEO / Acme / Dana.Both sources are available before decoding.
1 · encode tokensMLP and attention layers activate features for company facts, role questions, and answer style.Attention can point from the query tokens back to the context sentence.Weights supply the procedure for using context.
2 · retrieve evidenceThe model may retrieve a compressed prior: “Acme CEO = Sam.”Attention heads bind the current entity and role to the nearby answer token Dana.This is where RAG helps: retrieved text becomes inspectable context.
3 · compose answerThe Sam prior contributes positive logit mass to Sam.The Dana sentence contributes stronger logit mass to Dana.In the worked example: Dana 3.0 vs Sam 2.0.
4 · decodeIf context is weak, absent, or contradicted by many priors, the model may answer Sam.If context is clear and salient, the model answers Dana.The final answer reflects the balance, not a guaranteed rule.
5 · update choiceFine-tune if “Dana is CEO” should become a durable behavior across many future prompts.Use prompt/RAG if the fact is fresh, local, auditable, or likely to change.This is the deployment tradeoff.
When to update weights vs supply context

Update weights when you want a stable capability, style, policy, or recurring domain skill that should generalize across many situations and tolerate missing documents. Supply context when the information is fresh, source-grounded, user-specific, legally/auditably tied to documents, or likely to change. Most strong systems need both: weights learn how to reason and use evidence; context provides the particular evidence worth reasoning over today.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Parametric knowledgeInformation or skill encoded in the model’s weights through training.
In-context knowledgeInformation supplied as prompt tokens at inference time, usable without weight updates.
Reversal curseA failure where a model trained on “A relates to B” cannot answer the logically reversed query “what relates to B?”
Latent informationInformation implied by documents but not explicitly written as the target answer.
Syllogistic generalizationDeriving a conclusion from premises, such as “all X are Y” and “no Y are Z” implying “no X are Z.”
Episodic retrievalBringing specific past documents or experiences back into context at test time.
Train-time augmentationGenerating extra traces or conclusions before training so latent implications become explicit examples.
Test-time computeSpending extra inference steps, often reasoning tokens, to retrieve or compose useful information.
Knowledge conflictA situation where prompt evidence and parametric priors support different answers.
