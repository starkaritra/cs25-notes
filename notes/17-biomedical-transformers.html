Transformers in Biology & Medicine

Vivek Natarajan's lecture shows why biomedical AI is becoming a sequence-modeling field: clinical notes, patient histories, proteins, and genomes all contain long-range patterns that transformers can learn.

🎓 Vivek Natarajan ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A doctor's note is a sequence of clinical concepts. A patient's record is a sequence of encounters. A protein is a sentence in the language of life, written with amino-acid letters. A genome is a book-scale string of A/C/G/T symbols. If transformers are the architecture that made machines good at learning from sequences, then biology and medicine are not side quests — they are one of the most natural homes for the idea.

Running analogy

Think of biomedicine as a giant library with three shelves: clinical stories, protein sentences, and genomic books. Transformers are not magic doctors or biologists; they are powerful librarians that learn which words, residues, or bases tend to appear together, even when the relevant pages are far apart.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

Skim these before entering the biomedical library.

  • Amino acid / protein — amino acids are the alphabet; a protein is a folded chain of amino acids whose shape and function depend on the sequence.
  • DNA / RNA — DNA stores genetic instructions in A/C/G/T bases; RNA is a related molecule used in transcription and gene expression.
  • Masked language modeling — hide one token and train the model to predict it from context; this powers BERT-like text models and protein models such as ESM.
  • Tokenization — choose the units the model sees: words/subwords for clinical text, residues for proteins, bases or k-mers for DNA.
  • Embeddings — learned vectors representing tokens; nearby vectors often capture related biological or semantic roles.
  • Softmax — turns logits into probabilities: if logits are \(z_i\), then \(p_i=\exp(z_i)/\sum_j\exp(z_j)\).

1.Biomedical data is language-like

Natarajan opens with a shelf-sized printout of the human genome: more than three billion base pairs, printed across well over a hundred volumes. That image makes the first principle hard to miss: biomedicine is full of long sequences whose meaning depends on context.

1.1 The lecture's actual systems

The talk starts with clinical LLMs, then moves deeper down the biological stack. The core systems it covers are MultiMedQA and Med-PaLM for medical question answering; Performer-style long-context protein modeling and ProtNLM for protein annotation; DeepConsensus for sequence correction; and Enformer for gene-expression prediction from long DNA context. This note also places those systems beside nearby landmark models: ESM, AlphaFold2's Evoformer, BioBERT/PubMedBERT/ClinicalBERT, DNABERT, Nucleotide Transformer, scBERT, and Geneformer.

Key equation — one abstraction for many biomedical sequences

$$x=(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_L), \qquad h_{1:L}=\operatorname{Transformer}_\theta(e(x_1),\ldots,e(x_L)).$$

Here, \(x_i\) may be a word, diagnosis code, amino acid, DNA base, or gene token; \(e(x_i)\) is its embedding; \(L\) is sequence length; and \(h_i\) is the contextual representation the model learns for position \(i\).

Common misconception

“Calling proteins or genomes languages is only a cute metaphor.” The metaphor has limits — molecules are not English — but it is operationally useful: token context, co-occurrence statistics, long-range constraints, and masked-token objectives all transfer surprisingly well.

✓ Check yourself — why are long-range interactions unusually important here?

A residue far away in the linear protein sequence may touch another residue after folding. A DNA enhancer far from a gene in the linear genome may influence transcription through 3D looping. A patient note from months ago may change how today's symptom should be interpreted.

2.Clinical language models: from benchmarks to bedside caution

A patient asks, “Can I take this medicine while pregnant?” A useful answer must understand the question, retrieve medical knowledge, reason about risk, communicate uncertainty, and avoid harmful overconfidence. That is why the lecture begins with clinical question answering instead of a toy text benchmark.

2.1 MultiMedQA: the benchmark is part of the model story

The Google Health team built MultiMedQA by unifying several medical QA datasets: professional medical exam questions, research questions based on PubMed abstracts, and consumer health questions. Natarajan emphasizes that ordinary metrics such as accuracy, BLEU, or CIDEr miss the clinical stakes, especially for long-form answers.

Key equation — selective prediction with self-consistency

$$\operatorname{defer}(q)=\mathbb{1}\left[\max_a \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbb{1}(\hat a_k=a) < \tau\right].$$

For question \(q\), sample \(K\) chain-of-thought answers. If the most common answer receives less than threshold \(\tau\) of the votes, the system should defer rather than answer confidently.

2.2 Med-PaLM: align a knowledgeable model to a safety-critical domain

The lecture's key clinical result is not “LLMs are already doctors.” The result is narrower and more useful: general-purpose LLMs encode some clinical knowledge, performance improves with scale, and instruction prompt tuning can make long-form answers more aligned with medical norms using only hundreds of expert-written examples.

Nearby biomedical NLP systems use a related but smaller-scale recipe. BioBERT continues BERT pretraining on PubMed and PMC text; PubMedBERT trains from scratch on PubMed; ClinicalBERT adapts BERT to clinical notes. Large medical LLMs such as Med-PaLM aim at broader QA, summarization, coding, triage support, and patient-facing explanation — but only under careful evaluation and deployment constraints.

Why prompt tuning helped

If the base model already stores partial medical knowledge, the alignment problem becomes: “speak and reason like a cautious clinician in this task.” Soft prompt vectors are a compact learned instruction prefix; they are not human-readable, but they steer the frozen LLM toward safer response patterns.

Common misconception

“A high USMLE score means the model is ready for diagnosis.” Exam accuracy is useful but incomplete. Real clinical use needs calibration, source citation, privacy protection, workflow integration, clinician oversight, and evaluation for harm and bias.

✓ Check yourself — why did the team ask both clinicians and lay users to evaluate answers?

Clinicians can judge factuality, reasoning, missing content, harm, and bias. Lay users are often the end readers, so they can judge whether an answer is understandable, helpful, and actionable.

3.Protein language models: sentences that fold

Take the short sequence MKTAY. A biologist does not care only whether the letters look likely; she cares what the chain folds into, what it binds, whether a mutation breaks stability, and what function it may perform. Protein language models exploit the fact that evolution has already written a massive training corpus.

3.1 ESM and masked residues

ESM models from Meta AI, including work by Rives, Lin, and collaborators, train masked language models on millions of protein sequences. The training target is simple — recover hidden amino acids — but the representations learn signals useful for contact prediction, structure, stability, and function. In the library analogy, the model learns grammar from protein sentences before anyone labels every sentence with a 3D shape.

Key equation — masked-residue objective

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}}(\theta)=-\sum_{i\in M}\log p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\setminus M}).$$

\(M\) is the set of masked positions, \(x_i\) is the true amino acid at position \(i\), and \(x_{\setminus M}\) is the visible context. Minimizing this loss forces the model to learn which residues fit a biological context.

3.2 Structure and annotation: Performer, AlphaFold2, ProtNLM

The transcript highlights two protein papers. First, a Performer-style architecture approximates softmax attention with random features, reducing long-sequence attention cost while avoiding hard sparsity assumptions that may be wrong for proteins. Second, ProtNLM maps amino-acid sequences to natural-language protein descriptions, helping annotate millions of previously uncharacterized UniProt entries.

AlphaFold2 is the major neighboring system: its Evoformer uses attention over a multiple sequence alignment (MSA) plus a pairwise representation to help predict 3D structure. We should not overclaim that it is “just a language model”; it combines evolutionary alignments, pair features, geometry, and structure modules. But it shares the same deep idea: sequence context contains structural information.

Key equation — efficient attention sketch

$$\operatorname{Attn}(Q,K,V)=\operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \quad \leadsto \quad \phi(Q)\bigl(\phi(K)^\top V\bigr).$$

Standard attention compares all pairs of \(L\) positions, costing roughly \(O(L^2)\). Performer replaces the softmax kernel with feature maps \(\phi(\cdot)\), aiming for approximately linear scaling in \(L\).

✓ Check yourself — why can masked-residue prediction teach structure without seeing structures?

Evolution preserves residues that work together. If two positions co-vary because they contact or jointly determine function, predicting one from the rest rewards the model for representing that constraint.

4.Genomics transformers: reading the book-scale genome

Now the library shelf gets much bigger. Human DNA is billions of bases long, and many regulatory elements act far from the gene they affect. A CNN can expand its receptive field with layers, but a transformer can directly model long-range interactions when the architecture and positional encoding are built for the scale.

4.1 DeepConsensus: sequence correction as careful editing

DeepConsensus fits early in the sequencing pipeline. A PacBio machine reads a DNA molecule multiple times, producing noisy subreads and a circular consensus sequence (CCS). The transformer takes subreads, the CCS read, and instrument/context features, then predicts a polished sequence with higher accuracy. The lecture highlights that ordinary cross-entropy is brittle for insertions and deletions, so the paper uses an alignment-aware loss.

Key equation — why alignment matters

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{edit}}(\hat y,y)\approx \operatorname{EditDistance}(\hat y,y),$$

where \(\hat y\) is the predicted polished read and \(y\) is the high-quality assembly label. A single inserted base shifts every later position, so position-wise cross-entropy can punish many bases for one local error.

4.2 Enformer, DNABERT, and Nucleotide Transformer

Enformer predicts gene expression and other genomic tracks from roughly 200 kb of DNA sequence. It mixes convolutional layers with transformer blocks and relative positional encodings to model enhancer–promoter interactions over long genomic distances. The lecture's biological point is crucial: an enhancer can be far away in the linear genome but close in 3D folded chromatin.

Other DNA language models make the tokenization choice explicit. DNABERT uses k-mers such as ACGTA as tokens. Nucleotide Transformer scales masked modeling over nucleotide sequences from many genomes. These models can support regulatory-element prediction, variant effect prediction, and annotation — but the genome's length makes context windows, compression, and evaluation central design problems.

Key equation — gene-expression prediction as supervised sequence modeling

$$\hat y_{t,c}=f_\theta(\text{DNA}_{1:200{,}000})_{t,c}, \qquad \mathcal{L}=\sum_{t,c}\ell(\hat y_{t,c},y_{t,c}).$$

The model reads a long DNA window and predicts genomic signal \(\hat y_{t,c}\) at genomic bin \(t\) and assay/cell type \(c\). The loss compares predictions with experimental measurements such as expression or chromatin accessibility.

✓ Check yourself — why did Enformer need long context?

Most disease-associated variants are non-coding. They may change regulatory elements such as enhancers, and those enhancers can influence genes from far away along the DNA sequence. Long context helps the model connect distant regulatory sequence to expression.

5.Single-cell models and the high-stakes limits

The transcript does not dwell on single-cell systems, but they fit the same library pattern. A cell can be represented by which genes are expressed and at what levels; models such as scBERT and Geneformer treat genes or expression ranks as tokens to learn cell states, perturbation effects, and disease-relevant patterns.

Common misconception

“More biomedical tokens automatically means safer biomedical AI.” Scale helps representation learning, but labels can be scarce, biased, private, and distribution-shifted. In high-stakes domains, the evaluation and governance problem can be as hard as the modeling problem.

  • Tokenization choices: amino-acid residues, DNA bases, k-mers, diagnosis codes, gene tokens, or subwords each change what “context” means.
  • Long sequences: genomes and long proteins exceed ordinary attention windows; efficient attention, hierarchy, recurrence, retrieval, and compression matter.
  • Scarce or at-risk labels: medical labels are expensive, noisy, and tied to real people; open benchmarks remain small because data lives in privacy-protected silos.
  • Privacy and PHI: clinical models must not memorize or leak protected health information.
  • Distribution shift: hospitals, instruments, populations, and protocols differ; a model can fail outside its training environment.
  • Evaluation and safety: clinical systems need harm, bias, uncertainty, source citation, and human-in-the-loop evaluation, not only leaderboard accuracy.
✓ Check yourself — name two failures that are not captured by ordinary accuracy

A model can give a correct multiple-choice answer but omit a dangerous caveat in long-form advice; it can also perform well on one hospital's data but fail on another population or sequencing instrument.

6.Worked example — masked-residue prediction by hand

Numbers make the protein-LM mechanic click. Suppose the sequence is M K T [MASK] Y. The full amino-acid vocabulary has about 20 residues, but we will inspect a toy subset of four candidate residues for the masked position: A, G, L, and V.

Step-by-step with real numbers

The model outputs logits:

$$z=[3.0,1.5,0.5,1.0] \quad \text{for } [A,G,L,V].$$

Softmax first exponentiates each logit:

$$e^{3.0}=20.0855,\quad e^{1.5}=4.4817,\quad e^{0.5}=1.6487,\quad e^{1.0}=2.7183.$$

Add them:

$$Z=20.0855+4.4817+1.6487+2.7183=28.9342.$$

Now divide each exponentiated logit by \(Z\):

$$p(A)=20.0855/28.9342=0.694,\quad p(G)=4.4817/28.9342=0.155,$$

$$p(L)=1.6487/28.9342=0.057,\quad p(V)=2.7183/28.9342=0.094.$$

The model's most likely residue in this toy subset is A, with probability about \(69.4\%\).

✓ Check yourself — how does masked-LM training use this prediction?

During training, the true hidden residue is known. If the true residue were A, the loss for this position would be \(-\log 0.694\approx0.365\). If the true residue were L, the loss would be \(-\log 0.057\approx2.87\), strongly pushing the model to raise L's logit in similar contexts.

7.End-to-end trace — one protein through a protein LM

Now follow one concrete protein fragment all the way through a protein language model. Let \(L=128\) residues and hidden width \(d=768\).

StepWhat happensShape
0 · sequenceStart with a protein represented as residue letters, such as a 128-residue enzyme fragment.sequence of L=128 residues
1 · tokenizeMap each residue to an integer token; the vocabulary is the 20 standard amino acids plus special tokens such as mask/pad/start.128 tokens, vocab≈20-25
2 · embedLook up each token's vector and add positional information.128×768
3 · transformer ×NSelf-attention and MLP blocks repeatedly contextualize each residue using the whole sequence.128×768
4a · per-residue / pair headA structure-oriented head reads residue embeddings, or residue-pair features, to estimate contacts or structural constraints.contacts: 128×128 or per-residue labels
4b · pooled headFor function prediction, pool sequence information into one vector and classify the protein.768 → function logits
5 · outputThe system returns a property: likely masked residue, contact map, stability proxy, or function label.task-specific prediction
Why unsupervised sequence modeling learns biology

Evolution is the hidden teacher. Residues that interact physically or functionally tend to co-evolve: change one, and another may need to change too. A masked language model trained on millions of natural sequences is rewarded for internalizing those statistical constraints, so its embeddings become useful for structure and function even before explicit labels are added.
