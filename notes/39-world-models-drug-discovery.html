Multimodal World Models for Drug Discovery — Eshed Margalit (Noetik.ai)

Noetik.ai's bet: if a model can fuse tumor images, spatial omics, proteins, and perturbations into a predictive simulator, drug discovery can move from slow trial-and-error toward high-throughput in-silico experiments.

🎓 Eshed Margalit ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

Most cancer drug ideas fail late, after years of biology, animal studies, and clinical trials. Margalit's lecture asks a sharper question: before testing a therapy in a patient, can we build a world model of tumor biology that predicts how cells, neighborhoods, and tissue will respond to interventions?

This continues the theme from Note 17 on biomedical transformers, but shifts from “biology as sequences” to “biology as a spatial, multimodal, perturbable world.”

Running analogy — a tumor city simulator

Imagine a city planner with satellite photos, street-level sensors, census tables, and emergency drills. A single sensor misses too much. A useful simulator fuses them, then asks: “If we close this road, where does traffic go?” Here the city is a tumor microenvironment, roads are cell-cell signals, and the “road closure” is a drug or gene perturbation.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

You'll follow this note smoothly if these are fresh.

  • Transformers & tokens — a transformer processes a sequence of vectors; here tokens can be image patches, gene-expression bins, protein channels, or perturbation descriptors.
  • Multimodal fusion — combine different data streams early, late, or mid-network; see Note 31 for the vision-language version.
  • Embeddings & cosine similarity — vectors close in angle are semantically similar; this is how CLIP-like models align images and text, and how we score outcomes below.
  • Self-supervised masked modeling — hide part of the input and train the model to reconstruct it; in the talk, Noetik masks most gene tokens so the model must learn biology, not shortcuts.
  • Spatial omics & histology — spatial transcriptomics measures RNA locations in tissue; H&E histology is a cheap stained tissue image showing morphology.

1.Biology as a world model, not a static classifier

Margalit opens with a familiar macroscopic example: when you approach a chaotic Stanford roundabout, you use vision, sound, and prior warnings to predict what happens if you step forward. Noetik wants the microscopic analogue: given a patient's tumor tissue, predict what happens if we perturb the system with a therapy.

1.1 Translation vs disambiguation

The talk separates two reasons to combine modalities. Translation asks one modality to recover another: H&E morphology predicts spatial transcriptomics, much like text can generate an image. Disambiguation uses one modality to resolve what another cannot know: a cell's few visible gene markers may be ambiguous until its neighbors, morphology, or protein context identify the local state.

Key equation — the biological world model

$$\hat{s}_{t+1}=f_\theta(s_t, a_t, m_t), \qquad \Delta=\hat{s}_{t+1}-s_t.$$

Here \(s_t\) is the current biological state of a cell, neighborhood, or tissue; \(a_t\) is an action such as a drug, knockout, or simulated gene-expression shift; \(m_t\) is the set of observed modalities; and \(\Delta\) is the predicted change the scientist cares about.

Common misconception

“A world model is just a big classifier with a fancy name.” Not quite. A classifier maps a sample to a label. A world model must represent state, accept counterfactual actions, and make predictions that can be checked in wet-lab or clinical data.

✓ Check yourself — in the city analogy, which part corresponds to a drug?

The drug is the planned intervention: closing a road, changing a traffic light, or adding a bus lane. It is not another sensor; it is the action whose consequences the simulator predicts.

2.Multimodal transformer tools from the first half of the talk

Before entering cancer biology, Margalit tours multimodal ML designs. The common trick is to make unlike measurements compatible enough that attention, contrastive losses, or conditioning layers can exchange information.

2.1 Five fusion patterns

  1. Joint embedding spaces: CLIP and ImageBind learn one geometry where paired modalities are close and mismatched pairs are far.
  2. Raw concatenation: if channels align spatially, an RGB image and a depth map can be stapled into a 4-channel input.
  3. Cross-attention: one stream supplies queries while another supplies keys and values, letting modalities mingle mid-network.
  4. Token concatenation: once everything is tokens, append image, text, omics, or conditioning tokens into one transformer sequence.
  5. Adaptive LayerNorm: a conditioning modality predicts scale/shift/gate parameters that steer the primary transformer block efficiently.
Key equation — self-attention becomes cross-attention

$$\operatorname{Attn}(Q,K,V)=\operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V,$$

$$Q=XW_Q,\qquad K=YW_K,\qquad V=YW_V.$$

In self-attention, \(X=Y\). In cross-attention, \(X\) might be spatial-RNA tokens while \(Y\) is an H&E or neighborhood stream, so one modality asks questions of another.

Why tokens make transformers attractive for biology

Biology arrives as awkwardly mixed objects: images, sparse gene counts, protein panels, patient mutations, and perturbation metadata. Once each is projected into tokens, the transformer supplies a generic mixing operator without requiring every assay to look like a natural sentence.

✓ Check yourself — why did Margalit like adaptive LayerNorm?

It is parameter-efficient. A conditioning stream can steer a main transformer block through learned scale/shift/gate parameters instead of forcing a full cross-attention module at every layer.

3.Worked example — perturbation response as embedding similarity

Suppose the model embeds a current T-cell state as \(z\), embeds a candidate drug perturbation as \(p\), and predicts a post-perturbation embedding \(\hat{z}'=z+p\). We compare \(\hat{z}'\) with three candidate outcomes: exhausted cell, tumor-killing cell, and unrelated inflammatory state.

Step-by-step with real numbers

Cosine similarity scores alignment:

$$\cos(\hat{z}',c_i)=\frac{\hat{z}'\cdot c_i}{\|\hat{z}'\|\,\|c_i\|}.$$

Assume the three cosine scores are exactly the lecture-style toy scores requested here:

$$[s_1,s_2,s_3]=[0.30,0.85,0.50].$$

Use a temperature \(\tau=0.2\) to turn them into probabilities:

$$s/\tau=[1.50,4.25,2.50].$$

Exponentiate:

$$e^{1.50}=4.482,\qquad e^{4.25}=70.105,\qquad e^{2.50}=12.182.$$

Add the weights:

$$Z=4.482+70.105+12.182=86.769.$$

Normalize:

$$p=[4.482/Z,70.105/Z,12.182/Z]\approx[0.052,0.808,0.140].$$

The model predicts outcome 2: the perturbation most resembles the desired tumor-killing response.

✓ Check yourself — if outcome 3's cosine rose to 0.90, what would happen?

Outcome 3 would win. With scores \([0.30,0.85,0.90]\) and \(\tau=0.2\), scaled scores are \([1.5,4.25,4.5]\); softmax gives roughly \([0.027,0.435,0.558]\).

✓ Check yourself — why can multimodal fusion beat a single assay?

A single assay has blind spots. H&E may show morphology but not gene programs; spatial RNA may show transcripts but miss protein abundance; a cell's own tokens may be ambiguous without neighborhood context. Fusion lets one stream disambiguate another.

4.Noetik's tumor data stack: the sensors for the simulator

Noetik collects human tumor specimens, punches tiny roughly 1 mm tissue cores, and runs aligned assays on the same physical regions. Margalit emphasizes that one core can contain thousands of cells and millions of spatially localized measurements.

4.1 Why cancer is a transformer playground

The modalities in the talk are unusually rich and aligned:

  • H&E histology: cheap, common stained tissue images showing morphology.
  • 16-plex protein immunofluorescence: a multi-channel image where antibodies mark proteins such as T-cell, B-cell, and tumor markers.
  • Spatial transcriptomics: CosMx-style RNA detections with \((x,y,z,\text{gene})\) tuples; the talk mentions 1,000-gene panels and whole-transcriptome runs above 18,000 genes.
  • Whole-exome sequencing: patient mutation information that can later label clusters or explain biology.
  • Perturbation hypotheses: synthetic knockdowns, overexpression, or drug-like changes expressed in model input space.
Key equation — masked biological autoencoding

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{mask}}(\theta)=-\sum_{i\in M}\log p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\setminus M}, c),\qquad |M|/L\gtrsim0.9.$$

\(x_i\) is a gene-identity plus expression-level token for one cell; \(M\) is the masked set; \(c\) is context such as nearby cells or H&E morphology. Noetik uses very high masking so the model learns regularities of patient biology rather than reconstructing from nearly complete inputs.

4.2 Concrete models shown in the talk

Several Noetik examples instantiate the same pattern. A masked autoencoder predicts missing gene tokens for a single cell. A neighborhood model compresses the eight nearest cells through a bottleneck transformer and uses that context to improve predictions. An H&E-conditioned model masks all gene tokens at inference and predicts where tumor markers such as keratin 19 should appear from morphology alone. A protein-image model predicts multiplexed protein images while receiving other modalities as extra tokens.

The foundation-model recipe is: pretrain on large aligned tumor data with self-supervised objectives such as masking and contrastive alignment, then attach readouts for perturbation response, patient outcome, or therapeutic target discovery.

Why 90%+ masking matters

With mild masking, the task can be solved by local shortcuts: 999 genes nearly determine the last one. With aggressive masking, the model must infer cell type, state, and local biology from sparse clues and context — the same regime needed for counterfactual simulation.

✓ Check yourself — why does spatial alignment between assays matter?

It lets the model learn correspondences at the same tissue location. If an H&E patch, protein channel, and RNA point cloud refer to the same neighborhood, H&E can supervise transcriptomic translation and omics can disambiguate morphology.

5.End-to-end trace — one tissue sample through the pipeline

Follow one concrete sample: a non-small-cell lung tumor core with a candidate immunotherapy perturbation. The key state is the representation shape or output at each stage.

Why a shared latent space enables in-silico experiments

Once images, RNA, proteins, genetics, and perturbations live in one learned state space, a scientist can change one input arm — for example, knock down a gene in a synthetic neighborhood — and ask how the predicted tissue state moves. The latent space becomes the tumor-city simulator's coordinate system.

6.From predictions to drug discovery

The talk's drug-discovery claim is deliberately not “the model cures cancer alone.” It is a lower-latency hypothesis engine. Noetik can run billions of virtual cell simulations, then pass prioritized ideas into biologist review, in-house assays, and in-vivo mouse experiments.

6.1 Applications and failure modes

The hard parts are not only architectural. The talk repeatedly returns to data scale, assay heterogeneity, privacy-protected hospital silos, causal validation, generalization across patients and tissues, and the need for wet-lab loops that evaluate predictions outside the model.

ApplicationWhat the world model contributesReality check
Target identificationSimulate synthetic knockdowns or up-regulation and rank changes in cytotoxic or immunogenic markers.Requires wet-lab validation and mechanism-of-action work.
Patient stratificationCluster predicted biology to find the subset likely to respond to an existing or failed therapy.Clinical data can be noisy, small, biased, or missing responders.
Immunotherapy responsePredict whether perturbing the tumor immune microenvironment reactivates T-cell killing.The model sees snapshots of disease, not the full healthy immune history.
Experiment prioritizationSearch many hypotheses before expensive animal or clinical testing.An agent or scientist still needs to choose useful simulations.
Key equation — counterfactual target ranking

$$\operatorname{score}(g)=R\!\left(f_\theta(s, a_g, m)\right)-R\!\left(f_\theta(s, a_0, m)\right).$$

\(a_g\) is a gene-targeting intervention, \(a_0\) is the no-treatment baseline, and \(R\) is a readout such as predicted tumor immunogenicity or T-cell cytotoxicity. High positive score means “worth testing,” not “guaranteed drug.”

Common misconception

“If the simulator ranks a target highly, clinical trials become unnecessary.” Safety and efficacy in humans still have to be established. The near-term win is narrowing the funnel: better targets, better patient subsets, and fewer uninformative experiments.

What the Q&A added

Noetik is not only a model API company: Margalit described an in-house full-stack setup with data generation, machine learning, biologists, immunologists, and mouse models. He also flagged privacy, hospital data silos, missing healthy tissue, and cross-scale integration as open problems.

✓ Check yourself — why is causality harder than correlation here?

Paired patient data can reveal that marker patterns co-occur with response, but a drug changes a causal system. The model's counterfactual must be validated by perturbation screens, animal studies, or clinical evidence to show the intervention actually causes the desired shift.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
World modelA learned simulator that predicts future biological state under observations and interventions.
Tumor microenvironmentThe tumor plus nearby immune cells, stromal cells, signals, vessels, and tissue structure.
H&EHematoxylin and eosin staining: a common histology image showing tissue morphology.
Spatial transcriptomicsRNA measurements that retain where each transcript appeared in the tissue.
ImmunofluorescenceProtein imaging using fluorescent antibodies that bind selected markers.
Masked autoencoderA model trained by hiding most input tokens and reconstructing the hidden values.
Adaptive LayerNormA conditioning method where another modality predicts normalization scale/shift/gate parameters.
Counterfactual simulationA “what if we changed this?” prediction, such as knocking down a gene in a virtual neighborhood.
Patient stratificationFinding which patient subgroup is likely to respond to a therapy.
