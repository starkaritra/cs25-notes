Strategic Games: AI for Multi-Agent & Imperfect-Information Play

Games are clean laboratories for the hard part of intelligence: choosing actions when other agents are adapting, hiding information, negotiating, and sometimes trying to exploit you.

🎓 Noam Brown (Meta AI) ⏱ ~50 min ▶ Watch
Why this matters

A poker bot can spend months training, then act from a table in milliseconds. A human pro, facing one crucial hand, may pause for five minutes and improve the decision. The lecture's central claim is that this pause — test-time search, or spending extra compute at the moment of decision — can be worth far more than simply scaling the trained model.

Running analogy

Think of an agent as a chess-clock negotiator. Training is the giant binder of preparation it brings to the match. Search is what it does when the clock is running: reread the position, imagine what others will do, and choose a move or message that will survive contact with opponents.

Prerequisites — 30-second refresher

You'll follow this note smoothly if these are fresh. Each is one line; skim, and dive into any that feel shaky.

  • Nash equilibrium — a strategy profile where no player gains by changing alone; this is the stability target behind poker bots and exploitability.
  • Zero-sum vs general-sum — in zero-sum, one player's gain is another's loss; Diplomacy is general-sum because cooperation and betrayal both matter.
  • Expected value — payoff averaged over uncertain actions or hidden states; planning chooses actions with better expected consequences.
  • Self-play / RL — agents improve by playing against versions of themselves; powerful in two-player zero-sum games, brittle when human conventions matter.
  • MCTS — Monte Carlo tree search samples future branches to guide decisions; it powered Go systems, but CICERO used a different planning style.

1.Games as strategy labs

Imagine two test questions. In Go, the entire board is visible: the difficulty is the depth of the future. In poker, your cards are visible but your opponent's cards and plan are not: the difficulty is the future and the hidden present. Diplomacy adds one more twist: the action space includes natural-language promises.

1.1 Perfect vs imperfect information

Perfect-information games such as chess and Go let the agent search over visible board states. Imperfect-information games such as poker and Diplomacy force the agent to carry beliefs: What could the hidden cards be? Which ally is sincere? What does my message make others believe I will do?

Lecture arc

Noam Brown starts from poker search, compares it to AlphaGo Zero's MCTS, then uses that lesson to explain CICERO: a Diplomacy agent that combines strategic planning with transformer-based dialogue.

✓ Check yourself — classify the games before peeking

Chess and Go are perfect-information. Poker is imperfect-information because private cards are hidden. Diplomacy is imperfect-information and general-sum because private messages, intentions, alliances, and simultaneous moves are not fully observable.

2.Game-theory atoms

Before CICERO can talk, it must answer a simpler question: “If everyone can adapt to everyone else, what does a good action even mean?” Game theory gives the vocabulary.

2.1 Nash, mixtures, exploitability

A normal-form game lists each player's actions and payoffs. In this tiny zero-sum game, Row wants the numbers high and Column wants them low:

Row payoffColumn: HeadsColumn: Tails
Row: Heads+1-1
Row: Tails-1+1

No pure action is stable here. If both choose Heads, Column wants to switch; if they mismatch, Row wants to switch. The stable policy is mixed: each player randomizes 50/50.

Key equation — Nash condition

$$u_i(\sigma_i^*,\sigma_{-i}^*) \ge u_i(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i}^*) \quad \text{for every player } i \text{ and alternative } \sigma_i.$$

Here, \(u_i\) is player \(i\)'s expected payoff, \(\sigma_i^*\) is their equilibrium strategy, and \(\sigma_{-i}^*\) means “everyone else's strategies.” Nobody can improve by deviating alone.

Key equation — exploitability

$$\operatorname{exploit}(\sigma)=u_i(\operatorname{BR}(\sigma_{-i}),\sigma_{-i})-u_i(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i}).$$

\(\operatorname{BR}\) is a best response. In the lecture's poker plot, lower exploitability means a worst-case adversary can extract less value from the bot.

Common misconception

“Optimal” does not always mean “always take the strongest-looking move.” In strategic games, predictable strength can be exploited. Sometimes the correct strategy is a carefully chosen random mixture.

✓ Check yourself — why is 100% Heads bad in Matching Pennies?

If Row always plays Heads, Column always plays Tails and Row gets \(-1\) every time. Randomizing 50/50 removes that exploitable pattern.

3.Search plus learning: the AlphaZero lesson

The lecture's repeated empirical pattern is: a trained policy is useful, but letting it think at decision time can change the game. Brown reports that adding search to poker reduced distance from Nash by about 7× — comparable, in his extrapolation, to scaling the model roughly 100,000×.

3.1 AlphaZero and poker search

AlphaZero-style systems learn a policy/value network by self-play, then use MCTS during play. The network says which branches look promising; search checks those branches with extra compute. Brown points out that AlphaGo Zero without test-time MCTS falls far below its full strength.

Key equation — one MCTS selection sketch

$$a = \arg\max_a \left[Q(s,a)+c\,P_\theta(a\mid s)\frac{\sqrt{N(s)}}{1+N(s,a)}\right].$$

\(Q(s,a)\) is the value found so far, \(P_\theta(a\mid s)\) is the policy net's prior, \(N\) counts visits, and \(c\) trades exploitation for exploration. The formula is not the whole AlphaZero recipe; it captures the binder-plus-clock idea.

For poker, the cultural starting point was different: game theory and reinforcement learning rather than board-game search. Libratus scaled real-time search in heads-up no-limit poker; Pluribus later used depth-limited search to handle six-player poker with surprisingly modest training cost.

✓ Check yourself — in the running analogy, what is “search”?

It is the negotiator using the live chess clock to reason through the current position, rather than blindly reading the first move from the prep binder.

4.Imperfect information: why “just search the tree” breaks

In Go, a branch is a visible future board. In poker, a branch may depend on cards you cannot see and on what your bet reveals. Searching one guessed game tree can leak information into the plan and make the bot exploitable.

4.1 Regret minimization

A core tool for two-player zero-sum imperfect-information games is Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR). It repeatedly asks: “At this information set, how much better would action \(a\) have done, holding fixed the reach probability from opponents and chance?” Positive regrets become more likely next time.

Key equation — regret matching

$$\sigma_{t+1}(a)=\frac{R_t^+(a)}{\sum_b R_t^+(b)}, \qquad R_t^+(a)=\max(R_t(a),0).$$

\(R_t(a)\) is cumulative regret for not choosing action \(a\). If all regrets are non-positive, use a uniform distribution. CFR uses this local update across many information sets and converges to Nash in two-player zero-sum games.

Common misconception

“If the agent is smart, it should infer the true hidden state.” Sometimes the right goal is not certainty. It is a strategy that performs well across possible hidden states and does not reveal a pattern a worst-case opponent can farm.

✓ Check yourself — what guarantee does CFR rely on?

The standard convergence-to-Nash result is for two-player zero-sum games. Many-player general-sum settings like Diplomacy lose that clean guarantee.

5.Diplomacy and CICERO: language becomes an action

Diplomacy was the hard next target because support, trust, and simultaneous moves make language strategic. Players privately negotiate for 5–15 minutes, then submit orders at the same time. A message like “I’ll support you into Belgium” matters only if the final move actually matches it.

5.1 Intent-grounded language

CICERO combined board/action history and dialogue to predict what every player might do, refined those predictions with planning, converted plans into intents, and conditioned a dialogue model on those intents. The language model was not asked to invent the strategy from scratch; it was asked to express a plan.

Key equation — piKL as a sketch

$$\pi_i^* \approx \arg\max_{\pi_i}\; \mathbb{E}_{a\sim(\pi_i,\pi_{-i})}[u_i(a)]-\lambda\,\operatorname{KL}(\pi_i\,\|\,\pi_i^{\text{human}}).$$

The first term rewards high-value actions; the KL penalty keeps the policy near human conventions. \(\lambda=0\) resembles unconstrained self-play; very large \(\lambda\) resembles pure imitation.

Brown emphasizes why this matters for transformers: ordinary language modeling imitates human-like text, but strategic dialogue is intentional. CICERO's dialogue model receives text plus a serialized intended action, so “what to say” is grounded in “what I plan to do.”

✓ Check yourself — why not train Diplomacy from scratch with pure self-play?

It might invent non-English conventions or strategies that work against itself but fail with humans. In a cooperative-competitive human game, modeling human conventions is part of the task.

6.Limits and open questions

The lecture is not a victory lap claiming games are solved. It is an argument for a research direction: make inference-time computation more general, so models can spend compute where the decision is hard.

  • Many players and general-sum games: Nash-style guarantees become weaker; equilibrium selection and conventions matter.
  • Exploitability vs best response: a policy can look strong on average while being fragile to targeted adversarial play.
  • Dialogue value: CICERO's value model did not deeply condition on long-term dialogue effects, so it avoided deception by conditioning on truthful intent.
  • Compute trade-offs: search moves cost from training time to inference time; that is worth it for some domains and wasteful for others.
  • Generality: MCTS, poker search, and piKL are domain-shaped. The open question is a more general way to “think longer.”
Bitter-lesson framing

Sutton's lesson says scalable general methods win in the long run, especially learning and search. Brown's update is: modern AI has scaled learning aggressively; the next frontier is scaling search or planning at inference time.

✓ Check yourself — name one reason CICERO used truthful intents

Its value model did not fully understand the long-term trust cost of lying. Conditioning on truthful intents reduced strategically unsound or deceptive messages that the system could not evaluate reliably.

7.Worked example — one regret-matching step by hand

Numbers make the core mechanic click. Suppose our imperfect-information agent has three legal actions: fold, call, and raise. After many simulated updates, its cumulative regrets are:

Step-by-step with real numbers

$$R=[+3,-1,0].$$

Keep only positive regret:

$$R^+=[\max(3,0),\max(-1,0),\max(0,0)]=[3,0,0].$$

Normalize by the sum \(3+0+0=3\):

$$\sigma=[3/3,0/3,0/3]=[1,0,0].$$

The next strategy puts all probability on fold in this toy state, because it is the only action with positive regret. In a full CFR system, later counterfactual updates can move probability back to other actions.

✓ Check yourself — compute the distribution for regrets [2, 5, -3]

Positive regrets are \([2,5,0]\), sum \(7\), so the next distribution is \([2/7,5/7,0]\approx[0.286,0.714,0]\).

8.End-to-end trace — one imperfect-information decision

Now follow a single concrete Diplomacy-style turn all the way through. CICERO is France, privately talking to Turkey about Tunis while also planning orders.

0 · observationAgent sees the board, its own units, public action history, and private messages so far.France has fleet near Tunis; Turkey can contest it
1 · belief / predictionDialogue-conditioned action model predicts plausible orders for each player and what they expect France to do.anchor policies: Turkey may go for Tunis or pivot east
2 · planningpiKL refines the anchor policies by favoring higher-value actions while staying near human-like conventions.France prefers: take Tunis; steer Turkey toward Rome/Serbia
3 · strategyThe planner outputs an action distribution and an intent for France plus an intended action for Turkey.P(take Tunis)=0.72; intent: Turkey moves east
4 · sampled actionFrance commits to a candidate order sampled from the planned strategy.order: fleet to Tunis
5 · languageDialogue model receives the serialized intent and generates candidate messages consistent with it.“You can make progress on Rome; I need Tunis for now.”
6 · filteringGrounding and value filters remove nonsense, contradictions, or messages that would cause low expected value.send message only if it does not trigger defensive response
7 · outcomeAll players submit simultaneous orders; the revealed result updates future beliefs and trust.new board + new dialogue context
What the trace reveals

The agent is not “chatting” and then “playing” as separate tasks. In CICERO, language is a strategic action whose quality depends on beliefs, plans, and expected responses.

Glossary

TermPlain meaning
Nash equilibriumA stable set of strategies where no one benefits by changing alone.
Mixed strategyA probability distribution over actions, used to avoid predictable exploitation.
ExploitabilityHow much a best-response adversary can gain against a strategy.
Information setStates an imperfect-information player cannot distinguish from their own observations.
CFRCounterfactual Regret Minimization, a regret-based algorithm for imperfect-information games.
Search / planningExtra computation at decision time to evaluate possible futures or responses.
piKLCICERO's planning method: self-play-like improvement regularized toward human imitation with a KL penalty.
IntentA planned action for the speaker and/or partner, serialized so the dialogue model can generate grounded messages.
Grounded dialogueLanguage tied to the actual world state and intended actions, not free-floating chitchat.
